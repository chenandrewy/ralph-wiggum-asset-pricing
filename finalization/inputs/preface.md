# Preface

This preface is almost entirely human-written. Please forgive typos and errors.

- Andrew Chen, April 9, 2026.

## Background

On March 8, 2025, I thought I should write a paper about hedging the singularity.

I was worked up. I was using AI to prove theorems, do lit reviews, and vibe code in my daily life. Six months ago, I had thought each of these things is impossible.

What will happen in the next six years?! Will my entire job be replaced by AI? I had no idea.

But I did know that if there are huge AI disruptions, then tech stocks would most likely benefit. So if anything bad happens to my human capital, I could partially hedge. 

I asked a friend if he would be interested in working on this paper. Unfortunately, he was busy. So, I thought I should use AI to do it.

This project was inspired by [Novy-Marx and Velikov (2025)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w33363)  and [Chris Lu et al. (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.06292). These projects use AI to generate massive amounts of academic research. My goal differs in quality over quantity. I want to generate just one paper, but one paper that (I hope) people find is worth reading.

This project was also inspired by [Garleanu, Kogan, and Panageas's (2012)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0304405X12000621) (GKP's) beautiful model of innovation and displacement risk. I read Garleanu et al. back when I was a PhD student and it has stuck with me. 

Last, I drew from [Hadfield-Menell and Hadfield (2018)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.04268) and [Bengio (2023)](https://www.journalofdemocracy.org/ai-and-catastrophic-risk/), who apply ideas from economics to AI catastrophe risk. [Hadfield-Menell and Hadfield (2018)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.04268) explains the connection between incomplete contracting and AI alignment. [Bengio (2023)](https://www.journalofdemocracy.org/ai-and-catastrophic-risk/) frames AI catastrophe risk in terms of what I would call decision theory and human incentives---though the essay is written in plain English. 

### The First Draft (April 2025) and R&R Decision

The [first draft](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/chenandrewy/Prompts-to-Paper/master/manyout0408-pdf/paper-run05.pdf) was finished 4 weeks later (April 9, 2025). It comes with a [Github repo](https://github.com/chenandrewy/Prompts-to-Paper), in which `make-papers.py` writes a short disaster risk theory paper, by prompting LLMs. The paper was quite open about how it's AI-generated.

Sadly, it was rejected by arxiv.org:

> Our moderators determined that your submission does not contain sufficient original or substantive scholarly research and is not of interest to arxiv.

Fortunately, the Critical Finance Review offered me an R&R. 

## The April 2026 Paper Generation Process

The editor tasked me with rewriting (reprogramming?) the paper from scratch, based on what I've learned about AI since April 2025.

The revised paper is generated by the [ralph-wiggum-asset-pricing](https://github.com/chenandrewy/ralph-wiggum-asset-pricing/tree/main) Github repo. As before, my goal was to minimize human  involvement, and thus demonstrate AI displacement risk. I settled on a two step process:


```

Algo April 2026:
----------------
Step 1.  Human selects one of five AI agent-written drafts for initialization.
    - All drafts are based on the same human-written 
      paper specification (`paper-spec.md`)
    - Agents use supporting docs (key lit, economic background, etc.)
      for context.

Step 2.  A "Ralph loop" improves the paper repeatedly:
    (a) An agent generates `improvement-plan.md` based on previous test results,
       `paper-spec.md`, and supporting docs
    (b) An agent executes `improvement-plan.md`
    (c) Agents evaluate ("test") the improved paper and code 
    (d) If any test fails, go back to (a)
```

This "Ralph loop" draws from [Geoff Huntley's Ralph Wiggum](https://ghuntley.com/ralph/) method. Like [Ralph Wiggum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralph_Wiggum) (from [the Simpsons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Simpsons)), the agents don't seem to mind iterating on this loop, day and night. Though, unlike Ralph, the agents won't keep going unless you feed them money.

My initial goal was "human as *Clockmaker*": the human designs the Ralph loop, and runs it completely unattended. I thought: well, if the output is bad, I'll just add more tests! But after including 25 tests, I would still find random minor problems (e.g. Opus used SIC codes to characterize AI stocks, why?!), and the agents were eating through two $200/month Claude Code subscriptions already. So I added a minimal human intervention in the form of Step 1.

In retrospect, I should have done most of the algo with OpenAI Codex, which is much more cost effective (and rigorous).

Nevertheless, the [ralph-wiggum-asset-pricing repo](https://github.com/chenandrewy/ralph-wiggum-asset-pricing/tree/main) should offer an effective way for you to replace some of your own labor. You should be able to clone the repo, modify `paper-spec.md` and `config-ralph.yaml`, follow the instructions, and come back to a completed paper. However, instead "human as *Clockmaker*", it's probably smarter to follow Huntley's "human *on* the loop" method. This means checking in on the loop every once in a while, and tweaking the settings to keep it going in the right direction. That choice is yours.

I hope you find the repo useful.

### The Paper Specification

The [paper-spec](https://github.com/chenandrewy/ralph-wiggum-asset-pricing/blob/main/spec/paper-spec.md) is basically the paper in bullet points. For example, the main model is described as:

4. Main model: singularity with displacement risk.
    1. Infinite-horizon, discrete-time model.
    2. Two agents: A representative household, who is the marginal investor, and AI owners who hold private AI capital, including unborn or not-yet-marketable capital, and are not marginal investors in public stocks.
    3. Two publicly traded assets: AI stocks and non-AI stocks. AI stocks grow as a share of the economy with each singularity.
    4. The private AI capital and AI owners can also be thought of as unborn capital and future owners that do not yet exist, as in `spec/lit/GKP-2012.md`.
    5. The singularity may cause extinction, as in `spec/lit/Jones-2024-AERI.md`.
    6. The relationship between price/dividend ratios and the singularity probability is studied quantitatively in a table with compelling magnitudes. The interaction with extinction risk is examined too.

The agents *can* write a good main model without so much detail. But the results varied too much to be acceptable for my idealistic (nihilistic?) "human as *Clockmaker*" goal. 

For practical, "human *on* the loop" use, I would start with a more general spec. Then as you observe how the loop is going, tighten the spec to keep elements of the paper that you like. The repo is designed so that you can modify the spec, config, and tests, while the AIs keep working. 

### The Tests

For the past eight weeks, my life was like this: I would run the Ralph loop overnight, examine the output in the morning, and then get frustrated by the quality of the paper. This would lead to test updates and new tests.

I was hoping for just a few tests.  A test suite with only

- `factcheck-theory`
- `factcheck-narrative`
- `factcheck-code`
- `factcheck-lit`

would have been beautiful. But there so many more ways for a paper to be ugly. For example, none of these tests found the problem with this text:

> Part (iii) [of Proposition 2] captures the interaction: existential risk *attenuates* the sensitivity of AI valuations to singularity beliefs.

> If the singularity also accelerates growth Jones (2024), AI owners' resources could grow large enough to make the cost of compensating the household negligible---the friction becomes self-resolving. Section 4 quantifies this attenuation.

"this attenuation" should refer to how the friction is self-resolving, but Section 4 does not show this. Instead, Section 4 shows attenuation in valuations. Similar word games have caused widespread confusion in the anomalies literature (regarding ["replication"](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3604626)  and ["false discoveries"](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2206.15365)). This nightmarish passage led to the `factcheck-anaphora` test, which fails the paper if there are "anaphora resolution problems". 

Having many tests is overall a good strategy. Like [Ralph Wiggum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralph_Wiggum), the AIs are easily distracted, and breaking up tests into smaller tasks helps them focus. For example, `visual-figures` judges the figures together with the surrounding text, while `visual-figures-image-only` focuses only the figure images, and is better at catching purely visual issues. A human might do both at the same time naturally, but the AIs struggled with this.

I also had tests for quality. `theory-deadweight` kept the theory streamlined. `theory-clarity` ensured important symbols are easy to find. `writing-intuition` ensures the intuition is described well. There are about 10 of these quality tests.

### Specialized Tests

The previous tests should be useful beyond my paper. I really do see researchers using Ralph loops regularly, very soon. I think we should be teaching Ralph loops to grad students. 

But in order to minimize human involvement in the final product, I created tests that target specific features of the paper. `element-opening-fig` and `element-transfers-fig` ensure the quality of the two key figures. `element-rhetoric-meta` ensured Claude would not over-emphasize the role of AI in writing the paper (the poor guy didn't know that humans strongly prefer human-written text). In practical work, these tests should be replaced with hands-on edits. 

The most critical specialized test is:

- `element-gkp-cites`

which instructs the AI to read [Garleanu, Kogan, and Panageas's (2012)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0304405X12000621), read the CFR referee report, and ensure the paper accurately represents GKP's ideas, while also being sensitive, gracious, and not-awkward in its citations. 

We scholars are much better than AIs at citing literature graciously. Naturally, our years of dedicated study and practice of carefully citing each other is hidden from the training data.

I guess that's our comparative advantage?

### Ralph's Work by Iteration

The Ralph loop runs on its own branch, and commits after each iteration. So you can see in painstaking detail how the paper progressed on the [ralph/run-final branch](https://github.com/chenandrewy/ralph-wiggum-asset-pricing/commits/ralph/run-final/).

Usually, the first 3-4 iterations catch the major issues. This was evident in the final run:

- `iter 1` fixed:
  - Missing core literature on displacement risk and asset pricing, especially Kogan-Papanikolaou line of work
  - Opening figure uses fabricated data
  - Inconsistencies between paper's displayed P/D formulas and code
  - Abstract/body mismatch on extinction risk distorting AI development
  - Model inconsistency in treatment of private AI capital dividends and unused risk-free-rate notation
  - Abstract exceeding 100-word limit
  - Missing clarification that paper not model GKP-style entry of future innovators

- `iter 2` fixed:
  - Overstatement of what GKP say about broader trading solving displacement risk
  - Introduction overusing AI-authorship device in a repetitive, awkward way
  - Verbal claims around valuation spread and Proposition 3 proof not matching paper's own results
  - Appendix/proof material being mostly ceremonial rather than doing real argumentative work

- `iter 3` fixed:
  - Lit review exceeding half-page spec limit
  - Wrong attribution of JPE 2020 displacement/inequality paper and inconsistent terminology in transfer section
  - Long proof of Proposition 1 appearing inline instead of being moved to appendix
  - Figure 2 text being too small to read
  - Colored hyperlink boxes showing up in compiled PDF

- `iter 4` fixed:
  - Opening figure still using fabricated data
  - Transfer figure not clearly showing that households are badly harmed with zero transfers
  - Citation errors around Kogan-Papanikolaou-related authorship and overly favorable framing of Nordhaus
  - Missing existence condition for Proposition 1, which made some Extension 2 valuations mathematically undefined
  
After the `iter 4` fixes, the remaining test failures were:
- `element-gkp-cites` Mischaracterization of GKP’s mechanism, inaccurate/insensitive "inessential extension" language
- `element-lit-review` Missing Knesl from lit review
- `factcheck-bysection` Text says non-AI dividends "shrink" but they only shrink relative to the economy
- `factcheck-freely` Remaining bibliography problem around “Left Behind”
- `visual-figures-image-only` Figure-formatting issues
- `visual-pages` Float / page-layout issue
- `writing-intro` Unneeded filler paragraphs, misplaced key material, tonal break caused by AI-authorship disclosure, main argument hard to follow on a skim

These are minor, with the exception of `element-gkp-cites`. I totally would **not** put my name on a paper that mis-characterizes the key closely-related paper. `iter 5` fixed the problem.

The later iterations were focused on minor issues. For example: 
- `iter 10` 
  - Attempted but failed: Tighten intro flow
  - Fixed: Figure 2 readability, Prop 2 (iii) stationarity discussion

- `iter 11`
  - Attempted but failed: Fix extension figure whitespace
  - Fixed: Seru is not a co-author on Kogan-Papanikolaou-Stoffman 2020, negative dividend accounting problem, `writing-intro` flow and contribution clarity

Overall, it was unclear to me if after iter 10, it makes sense to keep running the loop. On my budget, I would stop around there, and intervene by hand.

## Comparison with April 2025

So much has changed. 12 months ago, the [paper generation algo](https://github.com/chenandrewy/Prompts-to-Paper/) was pure gimmick. Back then, it was a sequence of LLM chat [prompts](https://github.com/chenandrewy/Prompts-to-Paper/blob/master/plan0408-piecewise.yaml)

```

Algo 2025:
----------------
- LLM Prompt  1: draft the model description based on human-specified 
                 functional forms and motivations
- LLM Prompt  2: find the price/dividend ratio
- LLM Prompt  3: generate a table based on human-specified
                 parameter values
...             
- LLM Prompt 10: write the full paper, based on the prose generated 
                 by previous prompts
```
Basically, I held AI's hand as we walked step-by-step through writing the paper. Even with the hand-holding, I needed to run 5 papers and choose the best one, to avoid the occasional LLM error. It would have made so much more sense for me to use LLMs as a souped-up Google queries, and write the paper more-or-less the old fashioned way. In contrast, the current algo is something I [am currently using for research](https://github.com/chenandrewy/HumanxAI-ChenAY/commits/main/) and will continue to use in the future.

But some things haven't changed. 12 months ago, I complained:
> Sonnet often fails to recognize that an economic model does not capture an important channel. This is common (no model can capture everything). The standard practice is to dance gingerly around the channel in the writing. A decent PhD student can recognize this. But Sonnet cannot. 

This is still true, even with agentic Claude Opus. I used a `theory-unmodeled-channels` test to catch this problem, but it would have made much more sense to intervene on these delicate issues myself.

## Limitations of the Current AI (April 12, 2025)

Right now, AI is a cognitive power tool, or cognitive [heavy equipment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_equipment). It can plow forward on just about any kind of knowledge task. But it's difficult to wield with subtlety or artistry. 

The artisanal elements of research, like

- characterizing the economic insights
- making connections across disparate literatures
- turning dry statistics into compelling figures and text
- **responding to referees and editors**

these are still best done with humans *in* the loop.

That last element is important to me. My dream was to have the AIs respond to the CFR referee and editor. I attempted this in old versions of the `paper-spec`:

- 5. An extension of the main model addresses the referee's suggestions in `spec/CFR-R1-report.md`:
    - a. The singularity may cause extinction, following `spec/lit/Jones-2024-AERI.md`.
    - b. Consumption may become infinite (in our case, only for the AI owners).
    - c. Government transfer programs attenuate displacement risk, emphasizing the role of heterogeneity at the singularity (cf. `spec/lit/Jones-2024-AERI.md`). 

and also with a `referee-cfr-r1` agent:

```

You are a constructive referee at a top finance journal.

You do NOT accept or reject this paper. You provide suggestions to help 
the author improve it.

A prior referee (CFR R1) raised two concerns about an earlier draft. 
That report is at `spec/CFR-R1-report`.
```

`referee-cfr-r1` produced decent suggestions, but the author agents could not assemble the advice into a coherent and convincing response. The resulting extensions had inconsistent assumptions, theoretical dead ends, and weak-to-non-existent economic takeaways. 

Ultimately, I hard-coded the extension's details. For example, the final `paper-spec` specified
- d. Extension 2: Government transfers could attenuate the incomplete markets problem, despite their deadweight costs.
    - i. The ideal resolution of market incompleteness is to allow broader trading of AI capital. But as pointed out by `spec/lit/GKP-2012.md`, this capital may not yet exist, and thus this ideal solution has limits.
    - ii. Government transfers may help resolve this deeper incompleteness problem, as mentioned in `spec/lit/GKP-2012.md`. But they incur sizeable deadweight costs (waste, fraud, abuse) that scale with the size of the transfers, making this solution ineffective and unattractive in standard settings. 
    - iii. But in a singularity, with potentially infinite output growth (`spec/lit/Jones-2024-AERI.md`), the government solution is worth considering, even in the face of immense deadweight costs. This possibility is analyzed quantitatively.         

This kind of artisanal work: tying together distant ideas and weaving it into an interesting economic takeaway, based on a general direction provided by a referee---this seems quite out of reach of current AI. 

To put it another way, in April 2025 I speculated

> At some point, 2024-style economic analysis will be "on tap." You'll be able to go to a chatbot and ask "write me a paper about hedging AI disaster risk," and it will return you something like this paper (probably something much better). 

Now we do have economic analysis on tap. But what we can offer on tap is more like 2024 Finance Research Letters than 2024 Journal of Finance. At least right now.

## The Future of AI and Economics Research (Speculative)

What will happen if and when economics research is actually "on tap"?

"Economics on tap" could be a disaster for the economics labor market (could be). It certainly *will* be an extremely cheap substitute for at least some economists' labor. I suppose the questions is whether that will result in a strong substitution away from labor.

The optimistic argument is that AI also *complements* economists' labor. Perhaps, the number of economists will remain the same, but our research output increases in terms of both quantity and quality. 

But there are reasons why total research output is limited. Two key factors in academic publishing are attention and reputation ([Klamer and van Dalen 2001, J of Economic Methodology](https://repub.eur.nl/pub/6875/2001-0221.pdf)). Readers can only pay attention to so many reputable scholars. These scholars, in turn, can only pay attention to so may projects. 

I'm not saying that I *expect* a disaster for the economics labor market.  But even if it's highly unlikely, it's still a scenario that economists should consider.
