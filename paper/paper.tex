\begin{abstract}
\noindent AI stocks trade at remarkable valuations. We propose that hedging demand is a contributing factor. In our model, a representative household faces a negative AI singularity---a sudden advance in AI that devastates household consumption by displacing both labor income and non-AI dividends, while enriching AI owners who hold private, untradeable capital. Public AI stocks appreciate when AI advances, providing a partial hedge. In equilibrium, AI stocks command a valuation premium that increases with the probability and severity of the singularity. Incomplete markets are essential: if households could access private AI capital, the hedging amplification would vanish.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Why are AI stocks so expensive? The largest AI companies trade at price-dividend ratios well above the market average, and their combined market capitalization dwarfs that of many national stock exchanges. Some of this reflects expected growth in earnings as AI technology matures. But even after accounting for growth, AI stock valuations appear high by historical standards.

We argue that hedging demand is part of the explanation. If artificial intelligence advances suddenly and dramatically---devastating the incomes of ordinary investors while enriching the owners of AI technology---then public AI stocks provide valuable insurance. Investors bid up AI stock prices not only for the expected dividends, but for the protection these stocks offer against an AI-driven catastrophe. Financial market solutions to AI disaster risk have received surprisingly little attention; this paper offers a first step. We decompose the AI valuation premium into a cash-flow component and a hedging amplification component. The hedging channel is a distinct force that operates on top of growth expectations, with testable implications tied to perceived singularity risk rather than earnings forecasts.

We formalize this idea in a simple consumption-based asset pricing model. A representative household consumes dividends from public stocks and non-tradeable labor income, and faces the risk of a \emph{negative AI singularity}: a sudden event that devastates both labor income and non-AI dividends while enriching AI owners who hold private, untradeable capital. The household cannot buy into these private AI firms---markets are incomplete. But the household can invest in publicly traded AI stocks, which appreciate when AI advances. In equilibrium, this hedging demand pushes AI stock valuations above those of non-AI stocks.

Figure~\ref{fig:ai-valuations} documents the motivating fact: publicly traded AI stocks have consistently traded at substantially higher price-dividend ratios than the market portfolio, with the gap widening sharply after the emergence of large language models in 2023.

\end{figure}

Our main result is a closed-form expression for the price-dividend ratio of AI stocks. The AI valuation premium---the gap between AI and market portfolio valuations---is increasing in the probability of a singularity, and decreasing in the existing AI share of the household's portfolio. In the data, AI stocks trade at price-dividend ratios roughly 2 to 2.7 times the market average (Figure~\ref{fig:ai-valuations}). When the model incorporates differential pre-singularity growth of 3--5 percentage points---consistent with analyst consensus---and moderate singularity risk ($\lambda = 0.02$--$0.05$), it generates valuation ratios of 1.8--2.3$\times$, approaching the observed 2--2.7$\times$ gap (Table~\ref{tab:differential}). At these empirically relevant calibrations, the growth differential accounts for most of the premium, but the hedging channel contributes a distinct 13--23\%---a qualitatively different force driven by singularity risk rather than earnings expectations. To isolate the hedging mechanism, we first present a common-growth benchmark in which the hedging channel alone generates a 30\% premium (Table~\ref{tab:calibration}). Under more severe but plausible calibrations ($\gamma = 5$, $\phi_L = 0.35$), the hedging share rises substantially and can become the dominant force, exceeding 50\% of the premium (Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity})---the channel is first-order when risk aversion and labor displacement are jointly high. The 13--23\% baseline is a lower bound anchored to conservative labor displacement ($\phi_L = 0.20$); evidence from rapid LLM adoption in white-collar tasks since 2023 suggests $\phi_L$ may be substantially higher, pushing the hedging channel toward dominance.

Three features make the hedging channel economically important even when its quantitative share is modest. First, it is the \emph{only} component of the premium that depends on incomplete markets, so it is the part that policy can directly affect: the welfare gains from broadening market access ($\omega$ up to 3.4\%) flow entirely through this channel. Second, it generates qualitatively different predictions---the premium responds to singularity-risk shocks rather than earnings revisions---and a preliminary event study finds positive abnormal returns for AI stocks around singularity-risk events (Table~\ref{tab:event}). Third, identifying a new pricing mechanism is a contribution in its own right, even when that mechanism is not the dominant force at every calibration.

The hedging channel survives realistic consumption composition. AI dividends are only about 5\% of household income, but the singularity also displaces labor---the household's dominant income source (65\% of consumption)---so the consumption drop remains large and the hedging amplifier is undiminished. Incomplete markets are essential: if the household could invest in private AI capital, it could perfectly hedge singularity risk, driving $J$ to~1 and the hedging amplifier to~1. The hedging component would vanish, though the cash-flow premium would remain.

Augmenting the model with business-cycle risk shows that the hedging channel can coexist with high observed betas: cyclical exposure generates a positive expected-return differential while singularity risk inflates \emph{valuations}, though quantitative reconciliation of expected returns remains subject to the standard limitations of consumption-based models.

Our paper applies the displacement-risk framework of \citet{garleanu2012displacement} to AI singularity risk. Both models feature technology shocks that benefit some agents at others' expense under incomplete markets, but differ in two key respects. First, in GKP, future innovators are unborn; in our model, AI owners exist but hold private capital. Second, GKP's intergenerational friction is structural---as they note (footnote~14), only bequests or transfers could eliminate it---whereas ours is reducible through financial innovation, parameterized by a continuous market-access parameter~$\alpha$ (Section~\ref{sec:incomplete}).

In an extension, we incorporate two features of transformative AI emphasized by \citet{jones2024ai}. First, if the singularity may cause human extinction, AI stocks are worthless in the extinction state and the hedging premium falls. We calibrate this using Jones's VSL estimates. Second, Jones shows that rich and poor households view existential risk differently: the rich, with more to lose, are more concerned. In our model, if AI owners (the rich) are pessimistic about survival, they discount their private capital and may be more willing to sell it, loosening the incomplete-markets friction and reducing the hedging premium. This connects our model to the broader debate about the opportunities and risks of transformative AI \citep{korinek2024scenarios}.

Our paper also draws on the rare disaster literature \citep{rietz1988equity, barro2006rare, wachter2013can}, which shows that small probabilities of large consumption drops can have first-order effects on asset prices. The AI singularity is a disaster risk with asymmetric structure: it devastates some assets while benefiting others. This asymmetry generates hedging demand rather than a uniform increase in risk premia, connecting the disaster risk literature to the cross-section of stock valuations.

The rest of the paper proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{sec:model} presents the model. Section~\ref{sec:equilibrium} derives equilibrium valuations and the main results. Section~\ref{sec:extension} extends the model to incorporate existential risk and heterogeneous beliefs. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{A Model of Singularity Hedging}
\label{sec:model}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Environment}

Time is discrete and infinite: $t = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\,$. The economy has two types of agents: a \emph{representative household} and \emph{AI owners}. The representative household is the marginal investor and sets asset prices. AI owners hold private AI capital and are not marginal investors; they do not participate in public equity markets.

The household has constant relative risk aversion (CRRA) preferences:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:utility}
E_0 \sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \beta^t \frac{c_t^{1-\gamma}}{1-\gamma}, \quad \gamma > 1, \quad \beta \in (0,1),
\end{equation}
where $c_t$ is consumption at time~$t$, $\gamma$ is the coefficient of relative risk aversion, and $\beta$ is the subjective discount factor.

Two assets are publicly traded: an \emph{AI stock} and a \emph{non-AI stock}. These are Lucas trees that pay dividends $D_t^A$ and $D_t^N$ respectively. The household also receives non-tradeable labor income~$L_t$, and its consumption equals total income:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:consumption}
c_t = D_t^A + D_t^N + L_t.
\end{equation}
Define the \emph{AI dividend share} $s_t \equiv D_t^A / c_t$ and the \emph{labor share} $\ell_t \equiv L_t / c_t$. The non-AI dividend share is $1 - s_t - \ell_t$. Labor income is non-tradeable: there is no claim on~$L_t$ that the household can buy or sell.


\subsection{The AI Singularity}

The economy begins in a \emph{pre-singularity} regime. In each pre-singularity period, with probability~$\lambda \in (0,1)$, an AI singularity occurs. With probability~$1-\lambda$, the economy remains in the pre-singularity regime. Once the singularity occurs, the economy enters a \emph{post-singularity} regime permanently. The singularity is a one-time event.

In both regimes, all income streams grow at deterministic rates. The singularity modifies this growth asymmetrically. We set $g^L = g^N$---labor income and non-AI dividends grow at the same rate---so that the AI share is the only source of compositional drift.

\medskip
\noindent\emph{Normal transition} (probability $1-\lambda$ pre-singularity; probability~1 post-singularity):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:normal}
D_{t+1}^A = (1+g^A)\, D_t^A, \qquad D_{t+1}^N = (1+g^N)\, D_t^N, \qquad L_{t+1} = (1+g^N)\, L_t,
\end{equation}
where $g^A \geq g^N > 0$. Define the \emph{aggregate consumption growth factor} $G(s_t) \equiv s_t(1+g^A) + (1-s_t)(1+g^N)$. Because $g^L = g^N$, the ratio $\ell_t/(1-s_t)$ is constant and $G$ depends only on~$s_t$. When $g^A > g^N$, the AI share rises deterministically: $s_{t+1} = s_t(1+g^A)/G(s_t)$.

\noindent\emph{Singularity transition} (probability $\lambda$, pre-singularity only):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:singularity}
D_{t+1}^A = (1+\theta)(1+g^A)\, D_t^A, \quad D_{t+1}^N = (1-\phi)(1+g^N)\, D_t^N, \quad L_{t+1} = (1-\phi_L)(1+g^N)\,L_t,
\end{equation}
where $\theta > 0$ is the fractional jump in AI dividends, $\phi \in (0,1)$ is the fractional drop in non-AI dividends, and $\phi_L \in (0,1)$ is the fractional drop in labor income. AI displaces workers alongside non-AI firms, but the labor impact~$\phi_L$ need not equal~$\phi$.

The singularity benefits AI owners and their associated public stocks while devastating labor income and the non-AI economy. The household's consumption growth upon singularity, relative to normal growth, defines the \emph{consumption jump factor}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:jump}
J(s_t, \ell_t) \equiv \frac{(1+\theta)(1+g^A)\,s_t + (1-\phi)(1+g^N)(1-s_t-\ell_t) + (1-\phi_L)(1+g^N)\,\ell_t}{G(s_t)}.
\end{equation}

We say the singularity is \emph{negative} if $J < 1$. For our baseline calibration ($\theta = 0.50$, $\phi = 0.30$, $\phi_L = 0.20$, $s_0 = 0.05$, $\ell_0 = 0.65$), $J \approx 0.81$, so the singularity is clearly negative. The household's consumption falls by 19\%, driven primarily by the 20\% labor-income decline on its largest income component.

We present analytical results for the baseline $g^A = g^N \equiv g$, in which $s_t$ and $\ell_t$ are constant, $G = 1+g$, and $J$ simplifies to $s(1+\theta) + (1-s-\ell)(1-\phi) + \ell(1-\phi_L)$. Section~\ref{sec:calibration} extends to differential growth.


\subsection{Incomplete Markets}
\label{sec:incomplete}

The household is excluded from private AI capital. This is the source of market incompleteness: the household cannot buy equity in privately held AI firms, data assets, or proprietary algorithms. It can only access AI exposure through publicly traded AI stocks, which represent a fraction of the total AI economy.

If markets were complete, the household could perfectly hedge the singularity by holding private AI capital. The singularity would then be a redistribution between asset classes, not a consumption disaster. The hedging component of the AI valuation premium would vanish, though AI stocks would still trade above non-AI stocks due to their higher conditional dividend growth.

We parameterize the degree of market incompleteness with a market-access parameter $\alpha \in [0,1]$. At $\alpha = 0$, the household is fully excluded from private AI capital---the baseline case. At $\alpha = 1$, the household has full access and markets are complete. For intermediate values, the household acquires partial exposure to private AI capital, and its effective AI share rises to:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:effective-share}
\tilde{s}(\alpha) = s + \alpha\,\psi,
\end{equation}
where $\psi > 0$ measures the size of private AI capital relative to the household's baseline consumption. The consumption jump factor generalizes to $J(\tilde{s}(\alpha))$, and all pricing results carry through with $\tilde{s}$ replacing~$s$. Three forces sustain this friction: \emph{information asymmetry} (outsiders cannot evaluate proprietary AI technology, creating adverse selection that makes securitization difficult), \emph{control and incentives} (AI owners retain private capital to preserve governance rights, a standard agency friction), and \emph{regulatory barriers} (accredited investor requirements and securities regulation limit household access to pre-IPO capital). Unlike the intergenerational friction in \citet{garleanu2012displacement}---which is structurally irreducible---our friction is reducible in principle, generating the policy lever ($\alpha$) that GKP's setting lacks. The distinction is not merely theoretical: the $\alpha$-trajectory is observable through proxies such as public-to-total AI market capitalization and secondary-market trading volume, and it is plausibly increasing (Section~\ref{sec:calibration}).

One might object that AI owners also hold public AI shares, and their SDF assigns low marginal utility to the singularity state ($J_O > 1$). In practice, insiders are constrained sellers---lockup agreements and diversification mandates mean they set quantities, not prices---so the marginal investor remains the diversified household. To assess robustness, suppose a fraction~$\eta$ of public AI shares are priced by AI owners. The effective hedging amplifier becomes $(1-\eta)\,J^{-\gamma} + \eta\,J_O^{-\gamma}$, which remains well above unity for $\eta \leq 0.20$ at baseline (Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity}).

We present the main results for the baseline case $\alpha = 0$ and examine the effect of partial market access in Section~\ref{sec:calibration}.

\section{Equilibrium Valuations}
\label{sec:equilibrium}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Stochastic Discount Factor}

In this general equilibrium economy, prices are determined by the household's Euler equations. The stochastic discount factor (SDF) is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sdf}
M_{t,t+1} = \beta \left(\frac{c_{t+1}}{c_t}\right)^{-\gamma}.
\end{equation}

For the baseline $g^A = g^N = g$, the pre-singularity SDF takes two values:
\begin{equation*}
M_{t,t+1} = \begin{cases}
\beta(1+g)^{-\gamma} & \text{no singularity (prob.\ } 1-\lambda\text{)} \\[4pt]
\beta\bigl[(1+g)\,J\bigr]^{-\gamma} & \text{singularity (prob.\ } \lambda\text{)}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation*}

Because $J < 1$ (negative singularity), the SDF is \emph{high} in the singularity state---marginal utility surges when the household's consumption drops. Assets that pay well in this state are therefore valuable.

\subsection{Main Results}

Define the \emph{effective discount factor}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:a-def}
a \equiv \beta(1+g)^{1-\gamma}.
\end{equation}
We assume $a < 1$, which ensures finite valuations. In the post-singularity regime, there is no further risk, and both stocks have the same price-dividend ratio:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pd-benchmark}
\bar{v} = \frac{a}{1-a}.
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Equilibrium Valuations]
\label{prop:valuations}
In the pre-singularity regime, the price-dividend ratios of AI and non-AI stocks are:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:pd-ai}
v^A &= \bar{v} + \frac{\lambda\, a\bigl[J^{-\gamma}(1+\theta) - 1\bigr]}{(1-a)\bigl[1-(1-\lambda)a\bigr]}, \\[6pt]
\label{eq:pd-nonai}
v^N &= \bar{v} + \frac{\lambda\, a\bigl[J^{-\gamma}(1-\phi) - 1\bigr]}{(1-a)\bigl[1-(1-\lambda)a\bigr]},
\end{align}
where $\bar{v} = a/(1-a)$ is the benchmark valuation absent singularity risk, and $J = s(1+\theta) + (1-s-\ell)(1-\phi) + \ell(1-\phi_L)$ is the consumption jump factor.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The Euler equation $v^A = E_t[M_{t,t+1}(D_{t+1}^A/D_t^A)(1 + v_{t+1}^A)]$ expands to $v^A = (1-\lambda)\,a\,(1+v^A) + \lambda\,a\,J^{-\gamma}(1+\theta)(1+\bar{v})$. Solving for~$v^A$ and substituting $1 + \bar{v} = 1/(1-a)$ yields~\eqref{eq:pd-ai}. The derivation for~$v^N$ is identical with $(1-\phi)$ replacing $(1+\theta)$.
\end{proof}

Equations~\eqref{eq:pd-ai}--\eqref{eq:pd-nonai} decompose each stock's valuation into two components: the risk-free benchmark~$\bar{v}$ and a singularity adjustment. For AI stocks, the adjustment is proportional to $J^{-\gamma}(1+\theta) - 1$, which is always positive since $J^{-\gamma} > 1$ (high marginal utility in the disaster state) and $(1+\theta) > 1$ (AI dividends surge). AI stocks are valuable precisely because they pay well when the household is desperate.

\begin{proposition}[Hedging Premium]
\label{prop:premium}
AI stocks command a valuation premium over non-AI stocks:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:premium}
v^A - v^N = \frac{\lambda\, a\, J^{-\gamma}\,(\theta + \phi)}{(1-a)\bigl[1-(1-\lambda)a\bigr]} > 0.
\end{equation}
Moreover:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] The premium is strictly increasing in~$\lambda$ (singularity probability).
\item[(ii)] The premium is strictly decreasing in~$s$ (AI dividend share).
\item[(iii)] The relative premium $v^A / v^N$ is bounded: $v^A/v^N \in \bigl[1,\; (1+\theta)/(1-\phi)\bigr]$, with the lower bound at $\lambda = 0$ and the upper bound at $\lambda = 1$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Equation~\eqref{eq:premium} follows by subtracting~\eqref{eq:pd-nonai} from~\eqref{eq:pd-ai}. Positivity is immediate since every term is positive.

\emph{Part~(i).} Write the premium as $K \cdot \lambda / (1-a+\lambda a)$ where $K = a\,J^{-\gamma}(\theta+\phi)/(1-a) > 0$. The function $\lambda \mapsto \lambda/(1-a+\lambda a)$ has derivative $(1-a)/(1-a+\lambda a)^2 > 0$.

\emph{Part~(ii).} Since $\partial J / \partial s = \theta + \phi > 0$ and $J < 1$ with $\gamma > 1$, increasing~$s$ raises~$J$ and lowers~$J^{-\gamma}$, reducing the premium.

\emph{Part~(iii).} At $\lambda = 0$, both valuations equal~$\bar{v}$ and the ratio is~1. At $\lambda = 1$, $v^i = a\,J^{-\gamma} F^i / (1-a)$ where $F^A = 1+\theta$ and $F^N = 1-\phi$, giving the ratio $(1+\theta)/(1-\phi)$.
\end{proof}

The premium decomposes into two economically distinct forces:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:decomposition}
v^A - v^N = \underbrace{\frac{\lambda\, a\,(\theta + \phi)}{(1-a)\bigl[1-(1-\lambda)a\bigr]}}_{\text{cash-flow premium}} \cdot \underbrace{J^{-\gamma}\vphantom{\frac{1}{1}}}_{\text{hedging amplifier}}.
\end{equation}
The \emph{cash-flow premium} reflects the fact that AI dividends jump by $(1+\theta)$ while non-AI dividends drop by $(1-\phi)$ upon singularity. A risk-neutral investor ($\gamma \to 0$, so $J^{-\gamma} \to 1$) would pay this premium based on expected cash flows alone. The \emph{hedging amplifier} $J^{-\gamma}$ reflects that marginal utility is high in the singularity state ($J < 1$, $\gamma > 1$), so the household overpays relative to expected cash flows. At our baseline calibration ($J \approx 0.81$, $\gamma = 3$), $J^{-\gamma} \approx 1.92$---hedging demand roughly doubles the cash-flow-only premium.

AI stocks act as insurance: they pay high dividends in the state where the household suffers most. The premium grows with the perceived likelihood of a singularity and shrinks as the AI share~$s$ increases, because a larger AI share makes the singularity less devastating. There is a natural self-limiting mechanism: as AI stocks become more expensive and a larger fraction of the portfolio, the very risk that justifies their premium diminishes.

\begin{corollary}[Partial Market Access]
\label{cor:partial}
The hedging premium $v^A - v^N$ is strictly decreasing in the market-access parameter~$\alpha$. As market access broadens, the singularity becomes less devastating and the hedging motive weakens.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Since $\tilde{s}(\alpha) = s + \alpha\psi$ is increasing in~$\alpha$ and the premium is decreasing in the effective AI share (Proposition~\ref{prop:premium}(ii)), the result follows.
\end{proof}

Corollary~\ref{cor:partial} gives the paper's policy discussion a formal backbone: broadening access to private AI capital (increasing~$\alpha$) continuously reduces the hedging premium. Unlike the intergenerational friction in \citet{garleanu2012displacement}, which cannot be relaxed through financial innovation, our friction admits a natural continuous relaxation.

A falling premium could mean the household loses a valuable hedge rather than becoming better off. The next result resolves this ambiguity.

\begin{corollary}[Welfare Gain from Market Access]
\label{cor:welfare}
The consumption-equivalent welfare gain from increasing market access from $\alpha = 0$ to $\alpha > 0$ is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:welfare}
\omega(\alpha) = \left[\frac{1-a + \lambda\,a\,J\bigl(\tilde{s}(\alpha)\bigr)^{1-\gamma}}{1-a + \lambda\,a\,J(s)^{1-\gamma}}\right]^{\!\frac{1}{1-\gamma}} - 1.
\end{equation}
The gain is strictly positive and increasing in~$\alpha$: broader market access makes the household unambiguously better off.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The household's pre-singularity value function satisfies $V(c) = [c^{1-\gamma}/(1-\gamma)] \cdot W(\tilde{s})$, where $W(\tilde{s}) = [1-a + \lambda\,a\,J(\tilde{s})^{1-\gamma}]/[(1-a)(1-(1-\lambda)a)]$. The consumption-equivalent variation $\omega$ satisfies $(1+\omega)^{1-\gamma} W(s) = W(\tilde{s}(\alpha))$. Since $J$ is increasing in its argument and $1-\gamma < 0$, $W$ is decreasing in~$\tilde{s}$ (welfare improving), so $\omega > 0$.
\end{proof}


\subsection{Calibration}
\label{sec:calibration}

Table~\ref{tab:calibration} reports equilibrium valuations for a range of singularity probabilities. The preference parameters ($\beta = 0.96$, $\gamma = 3$) are standard in the asset pricing literature. Growth of $g^N = g^L = 0.02$ matches the long-run average for U.S.\ equities and labor income; the baseline sets $g^A = g^N$. The AI dividend share $s = 0.05$ reflects the weight of AI-related firms in U.S.\ equity market capitalization (approximately 15\% of equities) rescaled to total household income---roughly 35\% of which comes from equity payouts. The labor share $\ell = 0.65$ matches the aggregate labor share of household consumption. The singularity parameters ($\theta = 0.50$, $\phi = 0.30$, $\phi_L = 0.20$) imply that AI dividends jump by 50\%, non-AI dividends drop by 30\%, and labor income drops by 20\%---a severe but not catastrophic event in which household consumption falls by 19\%.

Each parameter can be grounded externally. The annual probability $\lambda = 0.01$--$0.05$ brackets expert survey hazard rates \citep{grace2024thousands}. The AI jump $\theta = 0.50$ reflects the private-vs-public split: private AI capital is roughly comparable to public AI market cap, so a singularity doubling total AI value implies $\theta \approx 0.50$ for public stocks. The non-AI drop $\phi = 0.30$ and labor drop $\phi_L = 0.20$ are consistent with task-exposure estimates \citep{eloundou2023gpts}, with $\phi_L < \phi$ reflecting partial labor complementarity. Because labor is the household's dominant income source ($\ell = 0.65$), even a 20\% decline drives most of the consumption drop ($J \approx 0.81$).

Absent singularity risk ($\lambda = 0$), both stocks have a P/D ratio of 11.9, the standard Gordon growth benchmark. As singularity risk increases, AI stock valuations rise sharply: at $\lambda = 0.02$, the AI P/D ratio is 16.6 versus 12.8 for non-AI stocks, a 30\% premium. At $\lambda = 0.05$, the premium reaches 54\%.

Table~\ref{tab:calibration} isolates the hedging mechanism by shutting down differential growth; it is the ``pure hedge'' benchmark. The 30--54\% premiums demonstrate that singularity risk alone generates economically meaningful valuations, but they do not close the full gap with the data: in Figure~\ref{fig:ai-valuations}, AI stocks trade at price-dividend ratios roughly 2 to 2.7 times the market portfolio. Table~\ref{tab:differential} presents the empirically relevant case with both channels active. Crucially, the hedging channel survives even though AI dividends are only 5\% of household consumption: the consumption drop is driven by labor displacement ($\phi_L = 0.20$ on the 65\% labor share), not by the AI portfolio weight.

\subsubsection*{Differential Pre-Singularity Growth}

The baseline assumes $g^A = g^N$, isolating the hedging channel. When $g^A > g^N$, both channels operate simultaneously. With differential growth, P/D ratios become time-varying as $s_t$ rises. The Euler equation yields a recursive formula: at each~$t$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:recursive}
v_t^A = (1-\lambda)\,a_t^A\,(1 + v_{t+1}^A) + \lambda\,a_t^A\,J(s_t, \ell_t)^{-\gamma}\,(1+\theta)\,(1 + \bar{v}_{t+1}^A),
\end{equation}
where $a_t^A \equiv \beta(1+g^A)\,G(s_t)^{-\gamma}$ is the asset-specific effective discount factor and $\bar{v}_{t+1}^A$ is the post-singularity continuation value. The structural decomposition into cash-flow and hedging components carries through at each~$t$.

At $\lambda = 0.02$ and growth differentials of 3--5 percentage points---consistent with analyst consensus---the model generates $v^A / v^N$ of 1.75 to 1.97. At $\lambda = 0.05$, the range rises to 2.06--2.29, approaching the observed 2--2.7$\times$ range. The hedging amplifier contributes 13--23\% of the combined premium; the remainder reflects growth. This interaction is derived from a single model, not an informal decomposition.

\subsubsection*{Measuring Market Access and Private AI Capital}

The market-access parameter~$\alpha$ and the private AI capital share~$\psi$ can be grounded in observable quantities. Three proxies track~$\alpha$ directly: (i)~the ratio of public to total AI market capitalization---currently around 40--50\%, given that major private AI labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, xAI) have combined valuations comparable to leading public AI firms; (ii)~household participation rates in venture and private-equity funds, which remain below 5\% of U.S.\ households but are rising with accredited-investor definition expansions; and (iii)~secondary-market trading volume for pre-IPO AI shares on platforms such as Forge and EquityZen, which has grown sharply since 2023. These proxies suggest $\alpha$ is small but increasing.

The baseline calibration of $\psi = 0.15$ can be assessed independently. As of 2024, the combined valuations of major private AI firms total roughly \$500--700 billion. U.S.\ aggregate household consumption is approximately \$19 trillion, giving $\psi \approx 0.03$--$0.04$ on a narrow definition. However, $\psi$ should also include AI-adjacent private assets (proprietary datasets, cloud infrastructure, robotics firms) and the expected growth in private AI capital; a broader measure plausibly reaches $\psi \approx 0.10$--$0.20$, bracketing our baseline. The $\alpha$-trajectory is not merely hypothetical: AI-focused ETFs, sovereign wealth fund allocations, and proposed regulatory changes are all plausibly increasing household AI exposure, making the welfare gains in Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity} a moving target rather than an abstract bound.

\subsubsection*{Level Effects on Non-AI Stocks}

Table~\ref{tab:calibration} reveals that non-AI stock valuations also \emph{rise} with singularity risk: at baseline, $J^{-\gamma}(1-\phi) \approx 1.34 > 1$, so the marginal-utility surge more than compensates for the dividend drop. This distinguishes our model from standard rare-disaster frameworks \citep{rietz1988equity, barro2006rare}, where disaster risk depresses all valuations uniformly; here, disaster risk raises valuations---just more so for AI stocks.


\subsection{Testable Implications and Preliminary Evidence}

A pure growth story has no such prediction---it ties the premium exclusively to earnings revisions. In the time series, the model also predicts that the hedging premium erodes as the AI share~$s_t$ rises with differential growth (equation~\ref{eq:recursive}): as $s_t$ grows, $J_t$ rises toward~1 and the hedging amplifier declines---a dynamic implication, not merely a comparative static.

\subsubsection*{Expected Returns and Business-Cycle Risk}

In the baseline model, the singularity is the only source of risk and AI stocks earn \emph{lower} expected returns than non-AI stocks. Since $E[M\,R^i] = 1$ for any asset~$i$, the risk-free rate is $R^f = 1/E[M]$ and the expected excess return satisfies:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:expected-return}
E[R^i] - R^f = -R^f \cdot \text{Cov}(M,\, R^i).
\end{equation}
AI stocks pay high returns in the singularity state, where marginal utility---and hence the SDF---is also high. This positive covariance implies a hedging discount:
\[
E[R^A] - E[R^N] = -R^f \cdot \text{Cov}\bigl(M,\, R^A - R^N\bigr) < 0.
\]
This appears to conflict with the data: AI stocks have high market betas, high volatility, and procyclicality---characteristics that in standard factor models command \emph{higher} expected returns.

To reconcile, we augment the baseline with i.i.d.\ mean-zero shocks to normal-period growth. In normal times, aggregate consumption growth is $(1+g)(1 + \sigma\epsilon_{t+1})$, where $\epsilon_{t+1}$ has mean zero and unit variance. AI dividends load on $\epsilon_{t+1}$ with cyclical factor $b^A > 1$, while non-AI dividends load with $b^N < 1$.

\begin{proposition}[Business-Cycle Augmentation]
\label{prop:cycle}
Augment the baseline model with i.i.d.\ normal-period shocks as described above. Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] The price-dividend ratios in Propositions~\ref{prop:valuations}--\ref{prop:premium} are unchanged to first order in~$\sigma^2$: the hedging premium is determined by singularity risk, not by business-cycle volatility.
\item[(ii)] The expected-return differential decomposes exactly as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:return-decomp}
E[R^A\!-\!R^N] \;=\; \underbrace{(1\!-\!\lambda)\,\gamma\,(b^A\!-\!b^N)\,\sigma^2\,R^f}_{\text{business-cycle premium}\;(>0)} \;+\; \underbrace{(-R^f)\,\lambda\;\text{Cov}_{\text{sing}}(M,\, R^A\!-\!R^N)}_{\text{hedging discount}\;(<0)}.
\end{equation}
\item[(iii)] The finite-valuation condition $a < 1$ is sufficient for existence of the joint equilibrium.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The augmented SDF is $M_{t,t+1} = \beta[(1+g)(1+\sigma\epsilon_{t+1})]^{-\gamma}$ in normal times and $\beta[(1+g)J]^{-\gamma}$ upon singularity. The P/D Euler equation, conditional on no singularity, gains a factor $E[(1+\sigma b^i \epsilon)/(1+\sigma\epsilon)^\gamma] = 1 + O(\sigma^2)$ for each asset~$i$, leaving equations~\eqref{eq:pd-ai}--\eqref{eq:pd-nonai} unchanged at first order. For expected returns, the covariance $\text{Cov}(M,R^i)$ separates by independence of $\epsilon_{t+1}$ and the singularity indicator: the normal-period contribution is $(1-\lambda)\gamma b^i \sigma^2/(1+v^i)$ to first order in~$\sigma^2$, and the singularity contribution is unchanged. Differencing across $i \in \{A,N\}$ yields~\eqref{eq:return-decomp}. The condition $a < 1$ ensures convergent P/D ratios; the $O(\sigma^2)$ perturbation preserves this for small~$\sigma$.
\end{proof}

With empirically motivated parameters ($b^A \approx 1.3$--$1.5$, $b^N \approx 0.9$, $\sigma \approx 0.02$), the business-cycle premium differential is approximately 5--7 basis points---small relative to the hedging discount of roughly 80 basis points at baseline ($\lambda = 0.02$). Matching observed return differentials would require $\sigma \approx 0.15$, a standard limitation of consumption-based models rather than a defect of the hedging channel. The channel's contribution is to \emph{valuations}---P/D ratios---where the consumption-based framework performs well, rather than to expected returns. Quantitative reconciliation of expected returns with observed betas remains orthogonal to the paper's main contribution. High realized AI returns also reflect a peso problem: in the dominant normal state, the hedging discount has not materialized, and upward revisions in perceived~$\lambda$ further inflate realized returns through capital gains.

\section{Extension: Existential Risk, Heterogeneous Beliefs, and Infinite Output}
\label{sec:extension}
%% ============================================================

The baseline model treats the singularity as devastating but survivable. Following \citet{jones2024ai}, we now extend the model in three directions: extinction risk, heterogeneous beliefs about existential risk, and infinite output.

\subsection{Extinction Risk}

Suppose that conditional on the singularity occurring, there is a probability~$\delta \in [0,1)$ that humanity goes extinct. In the extinction state, all consumption and dividends drop to zero. With probability~$1-\delta$, the economy survives and the outcomes are as in the baseline model.

Since both AI and non-AI stocks pay zero dividends in the extinction state, this state contributes nothing to asset valuations---it is infinitely bad for welfare but irrelevant for pricing.

\begin{proposition}[Extinction and the Hedging Premium]
\label{prop:extinction}
With extinction probability~$\delta$ conditional on singularity, the hedging premium becomes:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:premium-extinction}
v^A - v^N = \frac{\lambda\,(1-\delta)\, a\, J^{-\gamma}\,(\theta + \phi)}{(1-a)\bigl[1-(1-\lambda)a\bigr]}.
\end{equation}
The premium is linearly decreasing in~$\delta$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In the extinction state, $D_{t+1}^i = 0$ and $P_{t+1}^i = 0$, so this state contributes zero to the Euler equation. The singularity-state contribution to the P/D ratio is scaled by the survival probability~$1-\delta$. The rest of the derivation follows Propositions~\ref{prop:valuations}--\ref{prop:premium}.
\end{proof}

AI stocks cannot hedge extinction---all assets are worthless when everyone dies---so higher extinction risk shrinks the premium. Table~\ref{tab:extinction} calibrates this: even $\delta = 0.10$ reduces the premium by a tenth; at $\delta = 0.50$, it is halved. \citet{jones2024ai} calibrates VSL-based trade-offs between AI growth and existential risk; for $\gamma = 3$, society would tolerate at most 0.5\% annual extinction risk, at which level the hedging premium is nearly unaffected.

\subsection{Heterogeneous Views on Existential Risk}

\citet{jones2024ai} emphasizes that attitudes toward existential risk depend on wealth: the rich, with more to lose, are more reluctant to gamble on AI. In our setting, AI owners---who are rich---may perceive a higher extinction probability~$\delta_O$ than the household's belief~$\delta_H$. The household prices equities using~$\delta_H$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:extinction} applies), but $\delta_O$ affects whether the friction persists: pessimistic AI owners discount their private capital and become more willing to sell.

\begin{proposition}[Disagreement and the Friction]
\label{prop:disagreement}
Suppose that conditional on the singularity occurring and humanity surviving, the incomplete-markets friction resolves with probability~$\pi \in [0,1]$, where~$\pi$ is increasing in the AI owners' extinction belief~$\delta_O$. If the friction resolves, the household rebalances into private AI capital so that the singularity is consumption-neutral ($J = 1$); the dividend asymmetry ($\theta$, $\phi$) is absorbed by portfolio adjustment rather than by consumption. The hedging premium becomes:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:premium-disagree}
v^A - v^N = \frac{\lambda\,(1-\delta_H)\, a\,(\theta + \phi)\,\bigl[(1-\pi)\,J^{-\gamma} + \pi\bigr]}{(1-a)\bigl[1-(1-\lambda)a\bigr]}.
\end{equation}
The premium is decreasing in both $\delta_H$ and $\pi$ (and hence in $\delta_O$).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The singularity state splits into four sub-states: extinction ($\lambda\delta_H$, zero contribution), survival with friction ($\lambda(1-\delta_H)(1-\pi)$, hedging amplifier $J^{-\gamma}$), survival without friction ($\lambda(1-\delta_H)\pi$, hedging amplifier~1), and no singularity ($1-\lambda$, no premium contribution). In the friction-resolved state, the household rebalances so $J = 1$ but the cash-flow differential persists. Summing gives~\eqref{eq:premium-disagree}.
\end{proof}

Disagreement can partially unwind the friction: pessimistic AI owners discount their private capital, creating opportunities for the household to buy in. The cash-flow premium remains regardless.


\subsection{Infinite Output and Market Completeness}

If the singularity produces large output for AI owners, sharing with the household becomes cheap, and the incomplete-markets friction weakens. We microfound~$\pi$ through a simple screening game that derives friction resolution from primitives rather than assuming it.

\medskip
\noindent\emph{A bilateral-trade microfoundation.}
AI owners hold private capital of quality $q \in \{H, L\}$, drawn independently with $\Pr(q = H) = \mu$. High-quality capital generates post-singularity output $Y_O > 0$; low-quality capital generates $\xi Y_O$ with $\xi \in (0,1)$. The household cannot observe~$q$ and offers a pooling price $P = [\mu + (1-\mu)\xi]\,Y_O$. A high-quality owner sells if and only if $P \geq Y_O - d_0$, where $d_0 > 0$ is the owner's reservation discount reflecting governance and control rents. This requires:
\[
[\mu + (1-\mu)\xi]\,Y_O \geq Y_O - d_0 \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad Y_O \leq \frac{d_0}{(1-\mu)(1-\xi)}.
\]
Low-quality owners always sell. When $Y_O$ is small, the adverse-selection discount $(1-\mu)(1-\xi)Y_O$ exceeds the governance rent $d_0$, and high-quality owners refuse to trade---a standard lemons problem. But AI owners with large~$Y_O$ face a second force: their wealth is concentrated in a single technology, creating a \emph{diversification motive}. We model this as an increasing willingness to accept a discount: the reservation discount grows with output, $d(Y_O) = d_0 + \kappa Y_O$ for $\kappa > 0$, reflecting that concentrated owners with vast wealth gain more from portfolio diversification than from retaining control. The trade condition becomes $Y_O \geq d_0 / [\kappa + (1-\mu)(1-\xi)]$ for large~$Y_O$, which is eventually satisfied. This addresses the governance objection directly: AI owners with enormous output sell not because they need the proceeds for consumption, but because diversification becomes increasingly valuable relative to control rents.

The probability of friction resolution is therefore:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pi-micro}
\pi(Y_O) = 1 - \frac{d_0}{d_0 + \kappa Y_O}, \qquad Y_O > 0.
\end{equation}
As $Y_O \to \infty$, $\pi \to 1$ and the friction resolves. Define $d \equiv d_0/\kappa$; then $\pi = 1 - d/(d + Y_O)$, which is equivalent to $\pi = Y_O/(d + Y_O)$. The constant~$d$ aggregates the information friction (adverse selection through $\mu$, $\xi$) and the governance friction (control rents $d_0$) into a single sufficient statistic, with the diversification motive ($\kappa$) providing the countervailing force that drives resolution.

The super-linear growth condition---$Y_O$ growing faster than linearly in the severity parameter~$\theta$---ensures that friction resolution eventually dominates. This is economically grounded: compute scaling laws and increasing returns to AI mean that doubling the singularity severity more than doubles AI output. The qualitative results (hump shape, convergence) hold for any growth rate exceeding linear.

\begin{proposition}[Hump-Shaped Hedging Amplification]
\label{prop:hump}
Suppose $\pi$ is given by~\eqref{eq:pi-micro} and $Y_O$ is increasing in~$\theta$ with $Y_O/\theta \to \infty$ as $\theta \to \infty$. Define the \emph{hedging component} of the premium as the difference between the total premium~\eqref{eq:premium-disagree} and the cash-flow-only premium (obtained by setting $J^{-\gamma} = 1$):
\[
\Delta^{\text{hedge}} = \frac{\lambda\,(1-\delta_H)\,a\,(\theta+\phi)\,(1-\pi)\,(J^{-\gamma} - 1)}{(1-a)\bigl[1-(1-\lambda)a\bigr]}.
\]
Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] As $\theta \to \infty$, $\pi \to 1$ and $\Delta^{\emph{hedge}} \to 0$. The total premium converges to the cash-flow-only premium, which is monotonically increasing in~$\theta$.
\item[(ii)] For small~$\theta$ ($J < 1$), the hedging component is positive and initially increasing. For large~$\theta$ ($\theta > [(1-s-\ell)\phi + \ell\phi_L]/s$), $J > 1$ and $\Delta^{\text{hedge}} < 0$. The hedging component rises, falls through zero, and becomes negative before vanishing.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Part~(i):} For large~$\theta$, $J \approx s\theta$ so $|(\theta+\phi)(J^{-\gamma}-1)| \sim \theta$, while $(1-\pi) = d/(d + Y_O)$ and the super-linear condition ensures $(1-\pi)\theta \to 0$. Hence $\Delta^{\text{hedge}} \to 0$.

\emph{Part~(ii):} At $\theta = 0$, $J < 1$ and $(1-\pi) > 0$, so $\Delta^{\text{hedge}} > 0$. As $\theta$ rises past $[(1-s-\ell)\phi + \ell\phi_L]/s$, $J$ crosses~1 and $J^{-\gamma}-1$ turns negative. For large~$\theta$, $(1-\pi) \to 0$ by Part~(i), so $\Delta^{\text{hedge}} \to 0$ from below.
\end{proof}

The hump shape captures a key tension: hedging amplification is largest in an intermediate regime where the singularity is harmful enough to generate high marginal utility but not so extreme as to resolve the friction through the diversification channel. Unlike \citet{garleanu2012displacement}'s permanent intergenerational friction, ours is potentially self-resolving---the same event that generates hedging demand may also resolve the friction that amplifies it---but the cash-flow differential remains.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

This paper offers a simple explanation for the high valuations of AI stocks: hedging demand against a negative AI singularity. The AI valuation premium decomposes into a cash-flow component and a hedging amplifier that reflects high marginal utility in the disaster state. At empirical calibrations, the hedging channel contributes 13--23\% of the valuation gap, rising substantially under higher risk aversion or labor displacement---exceeding 50\% of the premium when both are jointly high. The 13--23\% range is a lower bound, anchored to conservative labor displacement; as AI adoption accelerates, this share is likely to grow. The channel is qualitatively distinct: it generates predictions tied to singularity risk rather than growth expectations, and a preliminary event study confirms that AI stocks earn positive abnormal returns around singularity-risk events that do not coincide with earnings news (Table~\ref{tab:event}). Incomplete markets amplify the premium; when markets are complete, the hedging amplifier collapses to one. With differential pre-singularity growth, the model generates valuation ratios approaching the observed 2--2.7$\times$ AI premium (Table~\ref{tab:differential}). Augmenting the model with business-cycle risk shows that the hedging channel can coexist with higher expected returns from cyclical exposure---the two forces operate on different margins---though matching observed return differentials quantitatively requires parameters beyond the consumption-based framework.

The model suggests that elevated AI stock valuations partly reflect a rational response to a genuinely new risk: a concentrated transfer of economic power to AI owners. To the extent that public AI stocks hedge this transfer, their high prices are insurance, not a bubble. The hedging channel is the only component of the premium that depends on incomplete markets---and therefore the only part that policy can directly affect. Broadening access to private AI capital---through securitization, public offerings, or regulatory reform---reduces the incomplete-markets friction and yields welfare gains of up to 3.4\% in consumption-equivalent terms, all flowing through the hedging channel. Financial inclusion in the AI economy is not only an equity concern but a pricing one.
