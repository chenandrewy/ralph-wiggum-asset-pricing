\begin{abstract}
\noindent AI stocks trade at remarkable valuations. We propose that hedging demand is a contributing factor. In our model, a representative household faces a negative AI singularity---a sudden advance in AI that devastates household consumption while enriching AI owners who hold private, untradeable capital. Public AI stocks appreciate when AI advances, providing a partial hedge. In equilibrium, AI stocks command a valuation premium that increases with the probability and severity of the singularity. Incomplete markets are essential: if households could access private AI capital, the hedging amplification would vanish and the premium would shrink to its cash-flow-only value.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Why are AI stocks so expensive? The largest AI companies trade at price-dividend ratios well above the market average, and their combined market capitalization dwarfs that of many national stock exchanges. Some of this reflects expected growth in earnings as AI technology matures. But even after accounting for growth, AI stock valuations appear high by historical standards.

We argue that hedging demand is part of the explanation. If artificial intelligence advances suddenly and dramatically---devastating the incomes of ordinary investors while enriching the owners of AI technology---then public AI stocks provide valuable insurance. Investors bid up AI stock prices not only for the expected dividends, but for the protection these stocks offer against an AI-driven catastrophe. Financial market solutions to AI disaster risk have received surprisingly little attention; this paper offers a first step. We decompose the AI valuation premium into a cash-flow component and a hedging amplification component, showing that incomplete markets roughly double the premium relative to expected cash flows alone.

We formalize this idea in a simple consumption-based asset pricing model. A representative household faces the risk of a \emph{negative AI singularity}: a sudden event that transfers a large share of economic output from the household to AI owners who hold private, untradeable capital. The household cannot buy into these private AI firms---markets are incomplete. But the household can invest in publicly traded AI stocks, which appreciate when AI advances. In equilibrium, this hedging demand pushes AI stock valuations above those of non-AI stocks.

Figure~\ref{fig:ai-valuations} documents the motivating fact: publicly traded AI stocks have consistently traded at substantially higher price-dividend ratios than the market portfolio, with the gap widening sharply after the emergence of large language models in 2023.

\centering

Dividends are measured as total shareholder payouts (dividends plus net buybacks), the standard empirical counterpart of dividends in a Lucas tree model. \label{fig:ai-valuations}
\end{figure}

Our main result is a closed-form expression for the price-dividend ratio of AI stocks. The AI valuation premium---the gap between AI and market portfolio valuations---is increasing in the probability of a singularity, and decreasing in the existing AI share of the household's portfolio. For a calibration with a 2\% annual singularity probability, AI stocks trade at a price-dividend ratio 29\% above non-AI stocks (Table~\ref{tab:calibration}). In the data, AI stocks trade at price-dividend ratios roughly 2 to 2.7 times the market average (Figure~\ref{fig:ai-valuations}). The model's hedging channel accounts for a meaningful share of this gap; a back-of-the-envelope decomposition shows that the remainder is consistent with an AI dividend growth premium of 3 to 5 percentage points---well within empirical estimates (Section~\ref{sec:calibration}). Under more severe calibrations, the hedging channel alone can account for most of the observed gap.

Incomplete markets amplify the premium. The decomposition in equation~\eqref{eq:decomposition} clarifies why: if the household could invest in private AI capital, it could perfectly hedge the singularity risk, driving $J$ to~1 and the hedging amplifier to~1. The \emph{hedging component} of the premium would vanish, though the cash-flow premium would remain---AI dividends still grow faster than non-AI dividends upon singularity. The premium exists at its current magnitude because the household is excluded from the most direct hedge---ownership of the AI technology itself---and must instead rely on the imperfect hedge provided by public AI stocks.

Our paper applies the displacement-risk framework of \citet{garleanu2012displacement} to the specific context of an AI singularity. The models share a common structure---technology shocks that benefit some agents at the expense of others, with incomplete markets preventing full risk-sharing---but differ in three ways. First, the \emph{source} of the friction differs: in GKP, future innovators have not yet been born, so current agents cannot trade with them; in our model, AI owners already exist but their capital is private and untradeable. Second, the \emph{policy implications} differ: GKP's intergenerational friction is structural and cannot be reduced by financial innovation, whereas our friction can be relaxed by broadening access to private AI capital through securitization, public offerings, or regulatory reform. As GKP note in their footnote~14, bequests or government transfers could eliminate their displacement risk entirely; in our model, intergenerational transfers do not help, but financial inclusion does. Third, we parameterize the degree of market incompleteness with a continuous market-access parameter~$\alpha$ (Section~\ref{sec:incomplete}), which provides a formal policy lever: as~$\alpha$ rises from zero to one, the hedging premium degrades smoothly to zero. This continuous relaxation has no natural analogue in GKP's OLG setting.

In an extension, we incorporate two features of transformative AI emphasized by \citet{jones2024ai}. First, if the singularity may cause human extinction, AI stocks are worthless in the extinction state and the hedging premium falls. We calibrate this using Jones's VSL estimates. Second, Jones shows that rich and poor households view existential risk differently: the rich, with more to lose, are more concerned. In our model, if AI owners (the rich) are pessimistic about survival, they discount their private capital and may be more willing to sell it, loosening the incomplete-markets friction and reducing the hedging premium. This connects our model to the broader debate about the opportunities and risks of transformative AI \citep{korinek2024scenarios}.

Our paper also draws on the rare disaster literature \citep{rietz1988equity, barro2006rare, wachter2013can}, which shows that small probabilities of large consumption drops can have first-order effects on asset prices. The AI singularity is a disaster risk with asymmetric structure: it devastates some assets while benefiting others. This asymmetry generates hedging demand rather than a uniform increase in risk premia, connecting the disaster risk literature to the cross-section of stock valuations.

The rest of the paper proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{sec:model} presents the model. Section~\ref{sec:equilibrium} derives equilibrium valuations and the main results. Section~\ref{sec:extension} extends the model to incorporate existential risk and heterogeneous beliefs. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{A Model of Singularity Hedging}
\label{sec:model}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Environment}

Time is discrete and infinite: $t = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\,$. The economy has two types of agents: a \emph{representative household} and \emph{AI owners}. The representative household is the marginal investor and sets asset prices. AI owners hold private AI capital and are not marginal investors; they do not participate in public equity markets.

The household has constant relative risk aversion (CRRA) preferences:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:utility}
E_0 \sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \beta^t \frac{c_t^{1-\gamma}}{1-\gamma}, \quad \gamma > 1, \quad \beta \in (0,1),
\end{equation}
where $c_t$ is consumption at time~$t$, $\gamma$ is the coefficient of relative risk aversion, and $\beta$ is the subjective discount factor.

Two assets are publicly traded: an \emph{AI stock} and a \emph{non-AI stock}. These are Lucas trees that pay dividends $D_t^A$ and $D_t^N$ respectively. The household holds all outstanding shares of both stocks, and its consumption equals total dividends:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:consumption}
c_t = D_t^A + D_t^N.
\end{equation}

Define the \emph{AI dividend share}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:share}
s \equiv \frac{D_t^A}{c_t}.
\end{equation}
In the pre-singularity regime (defined below), this share is constant over time.


\subsection{The AI Singularity}

The economy begins in a \emph{pre-singularity} regime. In each pre-singularity period, with probability~$\lambda \in (0,1)$, an AI singularity occurs. With probability~$1-\lambda$, the economy remains in the pre-singularity regime. Once the singularity occurs, the economy enters a \emph{post-singularity} regime permanently. The singularity is a one-time event.

In both regimes, dividends grow at a common deterministic rate~$g > 0$. The singularity modifies this growth asymmetrically:

\medskip
\noindent\emph{Normal transition} (probability $1-\lambda$ pre-singularity; probability~1 post-singularity):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:normal}
D_{t+1}^A = (1+g)\, D_t^A, \qquad D_{t+1}^N = (1+g)\, D_t^N.
\end{equation}

\noindent\emph{Singularity transition} (probability $\lambda$, pre-singularity only):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:singularity}
D_{t+1}^A = (1+\theta)(1+g)\, D_t^A, \qquad D_{t+1}^N = (1-\phi)(1+g)\, D_t^N,
\end{equation}
where $\theta > 0$ is the fractional jump in AI dividends and $\phi \in (0,1)$ is the fractional drop in non-AI dividends.

The singularity is an AI advance that benefits AI owners and their associated public stocks, while devastating the rest of the economy. The household's consumption growth upon singularity is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:jump}
\frac{c_{t+1}}{c_t} = (1+g) \, J(s), \qquad J(s) \equiv 1 - \phi + (\theta + \phi)\,s.
\end{equation}

We say the singularity is \emph{negative} if $J(s) < 1$, which holds when $s < \phi / (\theta + \phi)$. That is, the singularity devastates the household whenever AI stocks are a sufficiently small share of its portfolio. For our baseline calibration ($\theta = 0.50$, $\phi = 0.30$, $s = 0.15$), the threshold is $s < 0.375$, so the singularity is clearly negative.


\subsection{Incomplete Markets}
\label{sec:incomplete}

The household is excluded from private AI capital. This is the source of market incompleteness: the household cannot buy equity in privately held AI firms, data assets, or proprietary algorithms. It can only access AI exposure through publicly traded AI stocks, which represent a fraction of the total AI economy.

If markets were complete---if the household could freely trade claims on private AI capital---it could construct a portfolio that perfectly hedges the singularity. A household holding both public stocks and private AI capital would experience no consumption disruption from the singularity, because the gains on AI capital would offset the losses on non-AI assets. In that case, the singularity would be a redistribution between asset classes, not a consumption disaster. The hedging component of the AI valuation premium would vanish, though AI stocks would still trade above non-AI stocks due to their higher conditional dividend growth.

We parameterize the degree of market incompleteness with a market-access parameter $\alpha \in [0,1]$. At $\alpha = 0$, the household is fully excluded from private AI capital---the baseline case. At $\alpha = 1$, the household has full access and markets are complete. For intermediate values, the household acquires partial exposure to private AI capital, and its effective AI share rises to:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:effective-share}
\tilde{s}(\alpha) = s + \alpha\,\psi,
\end{equation}
where $\psi > 0$ measures the size of private AI capital relative to the household's baseline consumption. The consumption jump factor generalizes to $J(\tilde{s}(\alpha))$, and all pricing results carry through with $\tilde{s}$ replacing~$s$. We present the main results for the baseline case $\alpha = 0$ and examine the effect of partial market access in Section~\ref{sec:calibration}.

\section{Equilibrium Valuations}
\label{sec:equilibrium}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Stochastic Discount Factor}

In this general equilibrium endowment economy, prices are determined by the household's Euler equations. The stochastic discount factor (SDF) is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sdf}
M_{t,t+1} = \beta \left(\frac{c_{t+1}}{c_t}\right)^{-\gamma}.
\end{equation}

Pre-singularity, the SDF takes two values:
\begin{equation*}
M_{t,t+1} = \begin{cases}
\beta(1+g)^{-\gamma} & \text{no singularity (prob.\ } 1-\lambda\text{)} \\[4pt]
\beta\bigl[(1+g)\,J\bigr]^{-\gamma} & \text{singularity (prob.\ } \lambda\text{)}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation*}

Because $J < 1$ (negative singularity), the SDF is \emph{high} in the singularity state---marginal utility surges when the household's consumption drops. Assets that pay well in this state are therefore valuable.

\subsection{Main Results}

Define the \emph{effective discount factor}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:a-def}
a \equiv \beta(1+g)^{1-\gamma}.
\end{equation}
We assume $a < 1$, which ensures finite valuations. In the post-singularity regime, there is no further risk, and both stocks have the same price-dividend ratio:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pd-benchmark}
\bar{v} = \frac{a}{1-a}.
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Equilibrium Valuations]
\label{prop:valuations}
In the pre-singularity regime, the price-dividend ratios of AI and non-AI stocks are:
\begin{align}
\label{eq:pd-ai}
v^A &= \bar{v} + \frac{\lambda\, a\bigl[J^{-\gamma}(1+\theta) - 1\bigr]}{(1-a)\bigl[1-(1-\lambda)a\bigr]}, \\[6pt]
\label{eq:pd-nonai}
v^N &= \bar{v} + \frac{\lambda\, a\bigl[J^{-\gamma}(1-\phi) - 1\bigr]}{(1-a)\bigl[1-(1-\lambda)a\bigr]},
\end{align}
where $\bar{v} = a/(1-a)$ is the benchmark valuation absent singularity risk, and $J = 1 - \phi + (\theta + \phi)s$ is the consumption jump factor.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The household's Euler equation for the AI stock is:
\[
v^A = E_t\!\left[M_{t,t+1}\,\frac{D_{t+1}^A}{D_t^A}\!\left(1 + v_{t+1}^A\right)\right].
\]
In the pre-singularity regime, this expands to:
\[
v^A = (1-\lambda)\,a\,(1+v^A) + \lambda\,a\,J^{-\gamma}(1+\theta)(1+\bar{v}),
\]
where the first term covers the no-singularity path (dividend growth $1+g$, next-period P/D still $v^A$) and the second covers the singularity path (dividend growth $(1+g)(1+\theta)$, consumption growth $(1+g)J$, next-period P/D collapses to $\bar{v}$). Solving for~$v^A$ and substituting $1 + \bar{v} = 1/(1-a)$ yields~\eqref{eq:pd-ai}. The derivation for~$v^N$ is identical, replacing $(1+\theta)$ with $(1-\phi)$.
\end{proof}

Equations~\eqref{eq:pd-ai}--\eqref{eq:pd-nonai} decompose each stock's valuation into two components: the risk-free benchmark~$\bar{v}$ and a singularity adjustment. For AI stocks, the adjustment is proportional to $J^{-\gamma}(1+\theta) - 1$, which is always positive since $J^{-\gamma} > 1$ (high marginal utility in the disaster state) and $(1+\theta) > 1$ (AI dividends surge). AI stocks are valuable precisely because they pay well when the household is desperate.

\begin{proposition}[Hedging Premium]
\label{prop:premium}
AI stocks command a valuation premium over non-AI stocks:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:premium}
v^A - v^N = \frac{\lambda\, a\, J^{-\gamma}\,(\theta + \phi)}{(1-a)\bigl[1-(1-\lambda)a\bigr]} > 0.
\end{equation}
Moreover:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] The premium is strictly increasing in~$\lambda$ (singularity probability).
\item[(ii)] The premium is strictly decreasing in~$s$ (AI dividend share).
\item[(iii)] The relative premium $v^A / v^N$ is bounded: $v^A/v^N \in \bigl[1,\; (1+\theta)/(1-\phi)\bigr]$, with the lower bound at $\lambda = 0$ and the upper bound at $\lambda = 1$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Equation~\eqref{eq:premium} follows by subtracting~\eqref{eq:pd-nonai} from~\eqref{eq:pd-ai}. Positivity is immediate since every term is positive.

\emph{Part~(i).} Write the premium as $K \cdot \lambda / (1-a+\lambda a)$ where $K = a\,J^{-\gamma}(\theta+\phi)/(1-a) > 0$. The function $\lambda \mapsto \lambda/(1-a+\lambda a)$ has derivative $(1-a)/(1-a+\lambda a)^2 > 0$.

\emph{Part~(ii).} Since $\partial J / \partial s = \theta + \phi > 0$ and $J < 1$ with $\gamma > 1$, increasing~$s$ raises~$J$ and lowers~$J^{-\gamma}$, reducing the premium.

\emph{Part~(iii).} At $\lambda = 0$, both valuations equal~$\bar{v}$ and the ratio is~1. At $\lambda = 1$, $v^i = a\,J^{-\gamma} F^i / (1-a)$ where $F^A = 1+\theta$ and $F^N = 1-\phi$, giving the ratio $(1+\theta)/(1-\phi)$.
\end{proof}

The premium decomposes into two economically distinct forces:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:decomposition}
v^A - v^N = \underbrace{\frac{\lambda\, a\,(\theta + \phi)}{(1-a)\bigl[1-(1-\lambda)a\bigr]}}_{\text{cash-flow premium}} \cdot \underbrace{J^{-\gamma}\vphantom{\frac{1}{1}}}_{\text{hedging amplifier}}.
\end{equation}
The \emph{cash-flow premium} reflects the fact that AI dividends jump by $(1+\theta)$ while non-AI dividends drop by $(1-\phi)$ upon singularity. A risk-neutral investor ($\gamma \to 0$, so $J^{-\gamma} \to 1$) would pay this premium based on expected cash flows alone. The \emph{hedging amplifier} $J^{-\gamma}$ reflects that marginal utility is high in the singularity state ($J < 1$, $\gamma > 1$), so the household overpays relative to expected cash flows. At our baseline calibration ($J \approx 0.82$, $\gamma = 3$), $J^{-\gamma} \approx 1.81$---hedging demand roughly doubles the cash-flow-only premium.

AI stocks act as insurance: they pay high dividends in the state where the household suffers most. The premium grows with the perceived likelihood of a singularity and shrinks as the AI share~$s$ increases, because a larger AI share makes the singularity less devastating. There is a natural self-limiting mechanism: as AI stocks become more expensive and a larger fraction of the portfolio, the very risk that justifies their premium diminishes.

\begin{corollary}[Partial Market Access]
\label{cor:partial}
The hedging premium $v^A - v^N$ is strictly decreasing in the market-access parameter~$\alpha$. As market access broadens, the singularity becomes less devastating and the hedging motive weakens.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Since $\tilde{s}(\alpha) = s + \alpha\psi$ is increasing in~$\alpha$ and the premium is decreasing in the effective AI share (Proposition~\ref{prop:premium}(ii)), the result follows.
\end{proof}

Corollary~\ref{cor:partial} gives the paper's policy discussion a formal backbone: broadening access to private AI capital (increasing~$\alpha$) continuously reduces the hedging premium. Unlike the intergenerational friction in \citet{garleanu2012displacement}, which cannot be relaxed through financial innovation, our friction admits a natural continuous relaxation.

The 29--53\% hedging premium is a first-order contribution from a channel that operates independently of growth expectations. We now decompose the residual and examine the sensitivity of the premium to key parameters.

\subsubsection*{Growth and Hedging: A Decomposition}

The model assumes a common dividend growth rate across all stocks. In practice, AI firms' dividends are expected to grow faster than the market average. How much of the observed valuation gap reflects growth, and how much reflects the model's singularity channel?

The model premium itself has two components (equation~\ref{eq:decomposition}): a cash-flow premium reflecting the dividend growth differential upon singularity, and a hedging amplifier reflecting the household's willingness to overpay because marginal utility is high in the singularity state. At baseline, the hedging amplifier ($J^{-\gamma} \approx 1.81$) roughly doubles the cash-flow-only premium.

Treating the model's combined singularity channel and secular growth expectations as approximately multiplicative, the singularity channel generates a ratio $v^A/v^N = 1.29$ at $\lambda = 0.02$. In the data, the ratio is roughly 2 to 2.7. The residual attributable to differential growth expectations is therefore $2.0/1.29 \approx 1.6$ to $2.7/1.29 \approx 2.1$. In a Gordon growth model, these ratios imply an AI dividend growth premium of approximately 3 to 5 percentage points above the market average---well within the range of analyst consensus forecasts for major AI firms. The singularity channel and the growth channel are complementary: neither alone explains the full gap, but together they can account for the observed valuations.

%% ============================================================
\section{Extension: Existential Risk, Heterogeneous Beliefs, and Infinite Output}
\label{sec:extension}
%% ============================================================

The baseline model treats the singularity as devastating but survivable. Following \citet{jones2024ai}, we now extend the model in three directions: extinction risk, heterogeneous beliefs about existential risk, and infinite output.

\subsection{Extinction Risk}

Suppose that conditional on the singularity occurring, there is a probability~$\delta \in [0,1)$ that humanity goes extinct. In the extinction state, all consumption and dividends drop to zero. With probability~$1-\delta$, the economy survives and the outcomes are as in the baseline model.

Since both AI and non-AI stocks pay zero dividends in the extinction state, this state contributes nothing to asset valuations---it is infinitely bad for welfare but irrelevant for pricing.

\begin{proposition}[Extinction and the Hedging Premium]
\label{prop:extinction}
With extinction probability~$\delta$ conditional on singularity, the hedging premium becomes:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:premium-extinction}
v^A - v^N = \frac{\lambda\,(1-\delta)\, a\, J^{-\gamma}\,(\theta + \phi)}{(1-a)\bigl[1-(1-\lambda)a\bigr]}.
\end{equation}
The premium is linearly decreasing in~$\delta$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In the extinction state, $D_{t+1}^i = 0$ and $P_{t+1}^i = 0$, so this state contributes zero to the Euler equation. The singularity-state contribution to the P/D ratio is scaled by the survival probability~$1-\delta$. The rest of the derivation follows Propositions~\ref{prop:valuations}--\ref{prop:premium}.
\end{proof}

The result is intuitive: AI stocks cannot hedge extinction, because all assets are worthless when everyone dies. Higher extinction risk reduces the fraction of singularity states in which the hedge pays off, shrinking the premium.

\citet{jones2024ai} calibrates the value of a statistical life (VSL) to assess the trade-off between AI-driven growth and existential risk. In his framework, a year of life is worth approximately six times per capita consumption. With $\gamma > 1$, utility is bounded, and even infinite consumption generates finite utility---the marginal value of more consumption declines sharply. Table~\ref{tab:extinction} calibrates the hedging premium for a range of extinction probabilities at our baseline parameters. Even a 10\% conditional extinction probability reduces the premium by a tenth; at $\delta = 0.50$, the premium is halved.

\subsection{Heterogeneous Views on Existential Risk}

\citet{jones2024ai} emphasizes that attitudes toward existential risk depend on wealth: rich households have a higher value of life and are therefore more reluctant to gamble on AI, while poorer households are more willing to accept existential risk for the chance of dramatically higher living standards. In our setting, this heterogeneity maps naturally onto the two types of agents. AI owners---who are rich---may perceive a higher extinction probability than the representative household.

We capture this by allowing the AI owners' subjective extinction probability~$\delta_O$ to differ from the household's belief~$\delta_H$. The household continues to price public equities using its own belief, so the formulas in Proposition~\ref{prop:extinction} apply with $\delta = \delta_H$. But the AI owners' belief~$\delta_O$ affects whether the incomplete-markets friction persists.

When AI owners are pessimistic about survival ($\delta_O$ high), they place less value on their private AI capital---capital that may be worthless if extinction occurs. This makes them more willing to sell or share their capital with the household. In the limit, if AI owners believe extinction is nearly certain conditional on singularity, their private capital is nearly worthless to them, and the household could acquire it cheaply.

\begin{proposition}[Disagreement and the Friction]
\label{prop:disagreement}
Suppose that conditional on the singularity occurring and humanity surviving, the incomplete-markets friction resolves with probability~$\pi \in [0,1]$, where~$\pi$ is increasing in the AI owners' extinction belief~$\delta_O$. If the friction resolves, the household rebalances into private AI capital so that the singularity is consumption-neutral ($J = 1$); the dividend asymmetry ($\theta$, $\phi$) is absorbed by portfolio adjustment rather than by consumption. The hedging premium becomes:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:premium-disagree}
v^A - v^N = \frac{\lambda\,(1-\delta_H)\, a\,(\theta + \phi)\,\bigl[(1-\pi)\,J^{-\gamma} + \pi\bigr]}{(1-a)\bigl[1-(1-\lambda)a\bigr]}.
\end{equation}
The premium is decreasing in both $\delta_H$ and $\pi$ (and hence in $\delta_O$).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The singularity state splits into four sub-states. With probability~$\lambda\delta_H$, extinction occurs and both stocks pay zero, contributing nothing. With probability~$\lambda(1-\delta_H)(1-\pi)$, humanity survives and the friction persists: the SDF is $\beta[(1+g)J]^{-\gamma}$ and dividend growth is $(1+g)(1+\theta)$ for AI and $(1+g)(1-\phi)$ for non-AI, contributing $\lambda(1-\delta_H)(1-\pi)\,a\,J^{-\gamma}(\theta+\phi)/(1-a)$ to the premium. With probability~$\lambda(1-\delta_H)\pi$, humanity survives and the friction resolves: the household rebalances so $J = 1$ and the SDF is $\beta(1+g)^{-\gamma}$, but AI dividends still grow by $(1+\theta)$ and non-AI by $(1-\phi)$, contributing $\lambda(1-\delta_H)\pi\,a\,(\theta+\phi)/(1-a)$ to the premium---the cash-flow differential persists even though the hedging amplifier equals~1. Summing the two surviving-state contributions and solving gives~\eqref{eq:premium-disagree}.
\end{proof}

The prediction is that disagreement about extinction risk can partially unwind the incomplete-markets friction. If AI owners are sufficiently pessimistic---as Jones's analysis suggests the rich may be---they will discount their private capital heavily, creating opportunities for the household to buy in. This is a financial channel through which heterogeneous beliefs about existential risk feed back into asset prices: the more AI owners fear extinction, the weaker the hedging amplification, and the lower the AI valuation premium. The cash-flow premium, however, remains.


\subsection{Infinite Output and Market Completeness}

If the singularity produces large output for AI owners, sharing with the household becomes cheap, and the incomplete-markets friction weakens. We formalize this by microfounding~$\pi$ as a function of post-singularity AI output.

Let $Y_O > 0$ denote the scale of post-singularity output accruing to AI owners. Sharing capital with the household requires AI owners to pay a fixed cost~$k > 0$ (due to contracting, regulatory, or organizational frictions). AI owners share only if the cost is small relative to their output, giving:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pi-micro}
\pi(Y_O) = 1 - \frac{k}{Y_O}, \qquad Y_O \geq k.
\end{equation}
As AI output grows, $\pi \to 1$ and the friction vanishes.

\begin{proposition}[Hump-Shaped Hedging Amplification]
\label{prop:hump}
Suppose $\pi$ is given by~\eqref{eq:pi-micro} and the post-singularity AI output scale~$Y_O$ is increasing in the severity parameter~$\theta$. Define the \emph{hedging component} of the premium as the difference between the total premium~\eqref{eq:premium-disagree} and the cash-flow-only premium (obtained by setting $J^{-\gamma} = 1$):
\[
\Delta^{\text{hedge}} = \frac{\lambda\,(1-\delta_H)\,a\,(\theta+\phi)\,(1-\pi)\,(J^{-\gamma} - 1)}{(1-a)\bigl[1-(1-\lambda)a\bigr]}.
\]
Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] As $Y_O \to \infty$ (equivalently, as $\theta$ grows without bound), $\pi \to 1$ and the hedging component $\Delta^{\emph{hedge}} \to 0$. The total premium converges to the cash-flow-only premium, which is monotonically increasing in~$\theta$.
\item[(ii)] The hedging component is hump-shaped in~$\theta$: increasing for small~$\theta$ (where the singularity becomes more harmful and the hedging amplifier rises) and decreasing for large~$\theta$ (where friction resolution dominates and $J^{-\gamma} - 1$ is multiplied by a vanishing $(1-\pi)$).
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Part~(i):} As $Y_O \to \infty$, $\pi \to 1$, so $(1-\pi) \to 0$ and $\Delta^{\text{hedge}} \to 0$. The total premium~\eqref{eq:premium-disagree} converges to $\lambda(1-\delta_H)\,a\,(\theta+\phi)/[(1-a)(1-(1-\lambda)a)]$, which is increasing in~$\theta$. \emph{Part~(ii):} The factor $(J^{-\gamma}-1)(\theta+\phi)$ is increasing in~$\theta$, while $(1-\pi) = k/Y_O$ is decreasing in~$\theta$. For small~$\theta$, $\pi$ is bounded away from~1 and the first effect dominates; for large~$\theta$, $k/Y_O \to 0$ at a rate that overwhelms the polynomial growth of $(J^{-\gamma}-1)(\theta+\phi)$, provided $Y_O$ grows sufficiently fast (e.g., $Y_O = \bar{Y}(1+\theta)$ suffices). The hedging component therefore rises and then falls.
\end{proof}

The hump shape captures a key tension: the hedging amplification is largest in an intermediate regime where the singularity is harmful enough to generate high marginal utility but not so extreme as to trivially resolve the friction. Unlike the intergenerational friction in \citet{garleanu2012displacement}, which is permanent because the trading partners do not yet exist, our friction is potentially self-resolving---the same event that generates hedging demand may also resolve the friction that amplifies it---but the cash-flow differential remains.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

This paper offers a simple explanation for the high valuations of AI stocks: hedging demand against a negative AI singularity. The AI valuation premium decomposes into a cash-flow component---AI dividends grow faster than non-AI dividends upon singularity---and a hedging amplifier that reflects the household's high marginal utility in the disaster state. Incomplete markets are not the sole source of the premium, but they amplify it: when markets are complete, the hedging amplifier collapses to one and the premium shrinks to its cash-flow-only value. A decomposition of observed valuations shows that the singularity channel and differential growth expectations together can account for the AI valuation premium in the data.

The key friction is market incompleteness. Private AI capital is concentrated in the hands of AI owners---founders, venture capitalists, and early employees---who are not marginal investors in public markets. The representative household cannot access this capital directly and must rely on the imperfect hedge provided by public AI stocks. We parameterize this friction with a continuous market-access parameter~$\alpha$, showing that the premium degrades smoothly as access broadens.

Our model suggests that the current wave of AI investment reflects, in part, a rational response to a genuinely new risk. The AI singularity is not a generic innovation shock; it is a concentrated transfer of economic power to a small class of AI owners. To the extent that public AI stocks offer a hedge against this transfer, their elevated valuations are not a bubble---they are the price of insurance.

Looking forward, our analysis highlights the role of financial market design in managing AI risk. The market-access parameter~$\alpha$ provides a formal policy lever: broadening access to private AI capital---through securitization, public offerings, or regulatory reform---directly reduces the incomplete-markets friction and, with it, the hedging premium. The policy implication is that financial inclusion in the AI economy is not only an equity concern but also a pricing one: incomplete markets generate the very premia that make AI stocks appear expensive.
