# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

This paper proposes that hedging demand against a negative AI singularity contributes to the high valuations of public AI stocks. The model is clean, the closed-form results are correct, and the economic intuition is sharp. The incomplete-markets mechanism is well-motivated and the extensions (extinction risk, heterogeneous beliefs, infinite output) are handled with appropriate parsimony. However, the paper's narrative makes two claims that outrun the formal model. First, the "self-limiting mechanism"—whereby a rising AI share erodes the premium over time—is presented as a dynamic, testable prediction, but the model holds the AI share constant pre-singularity, so it is only a comparative static. Second, the decomposition of observed valuations into a singularity channel and a secular growth channel is done informally ("approximately multiplicative") rather than derived from the model. Both issues trace to the same modeling choice: assuming a common pre-singularity dividend growth rate for AI and non-AI stocks.

## Comments

1. **The common pre-singularity growth rate assumption (g^A = g^N = g) suppresses the paper's most interesting dynamics and forces a key quantitative claim outside the model.** In the current model, both dividends grow at rate g pre-singularity (eq. 4), so the AI share s is constant until the singularity hits. This creates two problems. (a) The "self-limiting mechanism" (Proposition 2(ii), Section 3.4) is described as a dynamic prediction—"as the AI share s of public equity rises, the premium should erode"—but s never rises in the model. It is a cross-sectional comparative static, not a time-series prediction. The testable implication in Section 3.4 therefore points to variation the model cannot generate. (b) The back-of-the-envelope decomposition on page 10–11, which attributes the residual between the model's 29% premium and the data's 2–2.7x gap to a 3–5pp AI growth differential, is informal. The claim that the two channels are "approximately multiplicative" is not derived; in a model with both differential steady-state growth and singularity risk, the interaction could be non-trivially super- or sub-multiplicative depending on how faster AI growth changes s over time and thereby feeds back into J and the hedging amplifier. **Recommendation:** Allow differential pre-singularity growth rates (g^A > g^N). This makes s a deterministic, rising process, gives the self-limiting mechanism genuine dynamic content, and—most importantly—lets the paper formally derive the combined premium from both channels rather than appealing to an informal decomposition. The model remains tractable: with deterministic pre-singularity s_t, the Euler equation yields a recursive but still closed-form P/D ratio (a standard result in dividend-share models). This single extension would resolve the narrative–model gap, strengthen the testable implications, and make the quantitative comparison with the data internally rigorous.

2. **The policy discussion around the market-access parameter α lacks a welfare quantification, weakening the paper's most distinctive contribution relative to GKP.** The paper correctly emphasizes that its friction is reducible (unlike GKP's intergenerational friction), and Corollary 1 shows the premium is decreasing in α. But the paper never answers the natural follow-up: how much does the household *gain* from an increase in α? The premium falling is a price effect; whether it reflects a welfare improvement depends on whether the household is better off holding cheaper AI stocks plus partial private-capital access, or expensive AI stocks without it. A consumption-equivalent welfare calculation—expected utility at α = 0 versus α > 0—would make the policy lever concrete and quantitative. This is straightforward given the existing structure: household expected utility is a function of the effective share s̃(α), and the welfare gain from increasing α can be expressed in closed form as a consumption-equivalent variation. Without this, the policy message ("financial inclusion reduces the premium") is ambiguous—reducing the premium could mean the household is better hedged (good) or that the hedge is less valuable because the risk is smaller (also good, but for a different reason). Separating these channels would sharpen the paper's most distinctive policy contribution.
