# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" presents a clean, well-motivated consumption-based model in which publicly traded AI stocks command a valuation premium because they partially hedge against a negative AI singularity. The core model (Sections 2--3) is elegant and the calibration is informative. However, two issues limit the paper's contribution. First, the quantity the paper calls the "hedging premium" conflates a genuine hedging channel (SDF amplification from marginal-utility surges) with a mechanical cash-flow channel (AI dividends simply grow faster than non-AI dividends upon singularity), and the paper never decomposes them --- this matters because the paper's thesis is specifically about hedging *demand*, not about differential expected returns. Second, the empirical decomposition that splits the observed AI valuation gap into "hedging" and "growth" components is a residual attribution that provides no independent evidence for the hedging channel; a top journal will want sharper identification. These are tractable issues, and addressing them would substantially strengthen the paper.

## Comments

1. **The "hedging premium" bundles hedging demand with a mechanical cash-flow differential, and the paper should decompose them.** The premium in Proposition 2 (eq 13) is $\lambda a J^{-\gamma}(\theta+\phi) / [(1-a)(1-(1-\lambda)a)]$. This is the product of two economically distinct forces: (a) the cash-flow differential $(\theta+\phi)$, which reflects the fact that AI dividends jump by $(1+\theta)$ while non-AI dividends drop by $(1-\phi)$ upon singularity, and (b) the SDF amplification $J^{-\gamma}$, which reflects that marginal utility is high in the singularity state because household consumption falls. Only (b) is hedging demand in the asset-pricing sense --- it is the reason the household is willing to overpay *relative to expected cash flows*. A risk-neutral investor ($\gamma \to 0$, so $J^{-\gamma} \to 1$) would still pay more for AI stocks because of (a), but that premium is just a fair-value reflection of higher conditional expected dividends, not hedging. For the baseline calibration ($J \approx 0.82$, $\gamma = 3$), $J^{-\gamma} \approx 1.81$, so the hedging amplification roughly doubles the premium relative to the cash-flow-only benchmark --- hedging IS quantitatively important, but the paper never isolates this. The decomposition also resolves a conceptual tension the paper currently has around complete markets: when $J = 1$ (complete markets), $J^{-\gamma} = 1$ and the hedging amplification vanishes, but the cash-flow premium remains positive. The paper says "the valuation premium would vanish" under complete markets (Section 2.3, Introduction), which is incorrect --- it is the *hedging component* that vanishes. Making this decomposition explicit would (i) sharpen the paper's main claim, (ii) give the calibration a clearer interpretation, and (iii) fix the complete-markets verbal claims without requiring any change to the math in Propositions 1--2.

2. **The empirical decomposition in Section 3.3 does not identify the hedging channel separately from growth.** The paper observes that the model generates a P/D ratio of $v^A/v^N = 1.29$ at $\lambda = 0.02$, while the data show a ratio of 2--2.7$\times$, and attributes the residual to an AI growth premium of 3--5 percentage points. This is an accounting residual, not an identification: by construction, any gap between the model and the data is labeled "growth," which makes the hedging channel unfalsifiable. A referee at a top journal will ask: what evidence distinguishes the world where AI stocks are expensive because of hedging demand from the world where they are expensive purely because of growth expectations? The paper would benefit from at least one of the following: (a) a time-series prediction --- the hedging premium should increase when proxies for singularity risk rise (e.g., AI capability announcements, automation displacement events), holding growth expectations constant; (b) a cross-sectional prediction --- among AI stocks, those with higher singularity beta (more exposure to the negative-singularity state) should command higher valuations beyond what growth differentials explain; or (c) integrating differential growth directly into the model (allowing AI dividends to have a higher baseline $g^A > g$) so that the calibration jointly pins down both channels rather than residually attributing one. Even a brief discussion of testable implications would significantly strengthen the paper's empirical relevance. The current decomposition is a useful back-of-the-envelope exercise, but it should not be presented as evidence for the hedging channel.
