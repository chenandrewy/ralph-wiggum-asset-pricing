# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" presents a clean, well-structured consumption-based asset pricing model in which AI stocks command a valuation premium because they hedge a negative AI singularity under incomplete markets. The core theory is sound, the decomposition of the premium into a cash-flow component and a hedging amplifier is elegant, and the paper is tightly written. The main model (Sections 2--3) is publication-ready in terms of economic logic and formal results. However, two issues weaken the paper's contribution at the margin and should be addressed before the paper can be considered for a top journal. First, the quantitative claim about business-cycle expected-return differentials does not match the formal theory at the stated parameter values---a calibration error already flagged by the theory-correctness test, but one whose fix may require rethinking how the expected-returns discussion is framed. Second, and more substantively, the microfoundation for friction resolution in Section 4.3 is too thin relative to the weight the paper places on the policy channel ($\alpha$, welfare gains, the GKP comparison); strengthening this link would materially improve the paper's contribution.

## Comments

1. **The friction-resolution microfoundation (Section 4.3) needs tightening.** The paper's most distinctive claim relative to GKP is that the incomplete-markets friction is "reducible through financial innovation," parameterized by $\alpha$ (Section 2.3) and microfounded via $\pi(Y_O) = 1 - d/Y_O$ (Section 4.3). This is the theoretical foundation for the policy discussion, the welfare result ($\omega$ up to 3.4%), and the paper's claim to be a "first step" toward financial market solutions to AI disaster risk. Yet the microfoundation is a single reduced-form equation with no derivation from primitives. Three concerns: (a) Section 2.3 lists three distinct frictions---information asymmetry, control/incentives, and regulatory barriers---but $\pi$ collapses all three into a single scalar that vanishes as output grows. Why would control and governance frictions (agency problems, incentive alignment) dissolve just because output is large? An AI owner with infinite output has *less* reason to sell equity, not more, unless the model specifies a motive (e.g., diversification, consumption smoothing for the owner). (b) The "super-linear growth" condition ($Y_O/\theta \to \infty$) is asserted as "natural" with one sentence of justification ("increasing returns to AI"). This is doing real work in Proposition 8---it drives the hump shape and the convergence result---and deserves either a derivation or a more careful discussion of when it holds and fails. (c) The adverse-selection discount $d$ is introduced as a constant, but in any standard adverse-selection model (e.g., Akerlof, Myers-Majluf), the discount depends on the information gap, which may itself change with the singularity. The authors should either derive $\pi$ from a simple bilateral-trade or securitization game between AI owners and the household, or significantly temper the policy claims that rest on it. A two-paragraph screening model where AI owners with private information about capital quality choose whether to sell at a pooling price would suffice and would give the paper a genuine policy microfoundation that distinguishes it from GKP on formal, not just verbal, grounds.

2. **The business-cycle expected-return calibration is incorrect and, once fixed, the expected-returns discussion may need reframing.** The theory-correctness test already identified the error: with $\sigma \approx 0.02$ (consumption-growth volatility), the business-cycle premium differential from equation (17) is approximately 5--7 basis points, not the claimed 2--3 percentage points. The fix is straightforward---either use return volatility ($\sigma \approx 0.15$) or revise the claim---but the choice has narrative consequences. If $\sigma$ is reinterpreted as return volatility, the proof of Proposition 5 (which relies on $\sigma$ being small for the first-order invariance of P/D ratios) needs revisiting, since $O(\sigma^2)$ terms are no longer negligible at $\sigma = 0.15$. If the claim is revised to 5--7 basis points, then the business-cycle premium no longer clearly dominates the hedging discount (~80 bp), and the paper's reconciliation story---"AI stocks earn higher expected returns overall from cyclical exposure while the hedging channel operates through valuations"---becomes quantitatively ambiguous rather than clean. The authors should recalibrate carefully and, if needed, reframe the expected-returns discussion to acknowledge that the model does not fully resolve the tension between the hedging discount and observed AI stock returns. An honest treatment of this tension (perhaps noting that higher-frequency return-based $\sigma$ is needed, with appropriate caveats about the consumption-based framework) would be more credible than the current clean narrative.
