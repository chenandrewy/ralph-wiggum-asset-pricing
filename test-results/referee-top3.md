# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

This paper proposes that hedging demand against a negative AI singularity contributes to the high valuations of publicly traded AI stocks. The model is clean, the closed-form results are elegant, and the decomposition of the AI premium into cash-flow and hedging-amplifier components is a genuine contribution. The extension incorporating extinction risk, heterogeneous beliefs, and endogenous friction resolution is well-integrated. Both the theory and the calibration are internally consistent. However, two substantive issues weaken the paper's quantitative claims and its connection to standard asset pricing evidence. Addressing these would significantly strengthen the paper's case for a top journal.

## Comments

1. **The endowment-economy assumption inflates the AI share and overstates the hedging channel's quantitative contribution.** The model sets household consumption equal to total public stock dividends ($c_t = D_t^A + D_t^N$), which makes the AI dividend share $s = 0.15$ (the approximate weight of AI firms in U.S. equity market capitalization). In practice, labor income is the dominant source of household consumption---roughly 60--65% of GDP---and total payouts from all public equities are a modest fraction of aggregate household income. If $s$ is recalibrated as AI dividends relative to total household consumption (including labor income, transfers, and non-equity wealth), it shrinks to perhaps 1--3%. At $s = 0.02$, the consumption jump factor rises to $J \approx 0.99$ and the hedging amplifier $J^{-\gamma}$ collapses nearly to 1, rendering the hedging channel quantitatively negligible. The paper's calibration implicitly assumes that equity dividends are the household's only income source, which is the standard Lucas-tree abstraction but one that matters enormously here because the entire mechanism depends on $s$ being large enough that the singularity meaningfully harms household consumption through the portfolio channel. The paper should either (a) incorporate labor income explicitly---for instance, by modeling non-AI labor income as a third "asset" that also declines upon singularity, which would preserve $J < 1$ while being more realistic---or (b) provide a careful argument for why the endowment-economy calibration of $s$ is the right one for the hedging question, mapping "AI dividend share" to a consumption-relevant concept rather than a market-cap concept. This is not merely a technical point: the calibration tables (Tables 1--2) are the paper's primary evidence that the hedging channel is quantitatively meaningful, and they rest on this assumption.

2. **The model's expected-return prediction conflicts with the empirical risk profile of AI stocks and deserves a more rigorous treatment than the peso-problem appeal.** The model predicts that AI stocks earn *lower* expected returns than non-AI stocks because they hedge the singularity state (positive covariance between the SDF and AI returns). Empirically, however, AI stocks have high market betas, high return volatility, and strong procyclicality---characteristics that, in every standard factor model, command *higher* expected returns. The paper's model generates the opposite prediction because the singularity is the only source of risk; deterministic growth outside the singularity means there is no business-cycle covariance at all. The "Insurance Puzzle" discussion (Section 3.4) acknowledges the tension but resolves it entirely through the peso problem and belief revisions---both of which are unfalsifiable in short samples. A top journal will want more. At minimum, the paper should embed the singularity risk within a model that also features standard aggregate consumption risk (e.g., i.i.d. or persistent shocks to $g$). In such a setting, AI stocks would face two opposing forces on expected returns: a positive risk premium from business-cycle exposure and a negative hedging discount from singularity insurance. The paper should demonstrate that the hedging channel survives---and characterize when it dominates---in the presence of conventional risk. Without this, the reader cannot assess whether the hedging premium is a first-order or second-order effect relative to standard risk compensation, and the expected-return prediction remains in tension with the data rather than being reconciled with it.
