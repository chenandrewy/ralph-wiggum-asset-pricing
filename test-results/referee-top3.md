# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" proposes that publicly traded AI stocks command a valuation premium because they partially hedge households against a negative AI singularity in the presence of incomplete markets. The model is clean, the closed-form results are correct, and the paper tells a coherent story. The writing is sharp and the paper is well-structured. However, the paper's quantitative contribution is unclear: the baseline calibration produces a 29% hedging premium while the data shows AI stocks trading at 2--2.7 times the market P/D ratio. The paper attributes the residual to "differential growth expectations" without any formal analysis. A top journal will ask whether the hedging channel is quantitatively first-order or a small residual on top of a growth story, and the paper currently cannot answer that question. Additionally, the incomplete-markets assumption---that AI owners completely abstain from public equity markets---is empirically stark and deserves a more graded treatment. Addressing these two issues would substantially strengthen the paper's contribution.

## Comments

1. **The paper needs to formally assess the quantitative contribution of the hedging channel relative to growth differentials.** The baseline model generates a 29% premium ($v^A/v^N = 1.29$ at $\lambda = 0.02$), but Figure 1 shows AI stocks trading at price-dividend ratios 2--2.7 times the market portfolio. The paper acknowledges this gap and attributes the remainder to "differential growth expectations," but this is asserted, not shown. A referee at JF/JFE/RFS will want to see one of the following: (a) an extended calibration that incorporates heterogeneous expected dividend growth rates across AI and non-AI stocks, showing that the hedging and growth channels are complementary and that together they can match the observed premium; (b) a back-of-the-envelope decomposition that computes the growth rate differential implied by the residual after removing the hedging premium, and then assesses whether that implied growth differential is empirically plausible; or (c) a sensitivity analysis across the key parameters ($\gamma$, $s$, $\phi$, $\theta$) showing that reasonable alternative calibrations can bring the model premium substantially closer to the data. Without at least one of these, the reader cannot judge whether the hedging channel is quantitatively meaningful---a 29% premium could be a first-order contribution or a rounding error relative to a growth story that generates a 150% premium on its own. This is the paper's central vulnerability: the model is elegant, but the claim that hedging demand is "part of the explanation" needs quantitative substantiation. A simple extension with heterogeneous growth rates ($g^A \neq g^N$) would nest the pure-growth benchmark and the pure-hedging benchmark, allowing the reader to see how the two channels interact.

2. **The incomplete-markets friction is too stark and would benefit from a partial-participation extension in the main model.** The assumption that AI owners "do not participate in public equity markets" is doing all the work, yet it is empirically counterfactual: the largest AI company founders (who are literally the "AI owners" in the model) are among the most significant participants in public equity markets, and their firms are publicly traded. The paper implicitly acknowledges that this is a spectrum---Section 4.3 models the friction dissolving as output grows---but this flexibility appears only in the extension. In the main model, the friction is all-or-nothing. A more compelling approach would be to parameterize the degree of market incompleteness in the baseline model (e.g., the household can access a fraction $\alpha$ of total AI capital, with $\alpha = 0$ recovering the current model and $\alpha = 1$ giving complete markets). This would yield a premium that degrades gracefully with market access, which is both more realistic and more useful for policy analysis. It would also give the paper's policy discussion (broadening access to private AI capital) a formal backbone: the paper could show how much the premium falls as $\alpha$ moves from its current level toward 1. This modification is small technically---it adjusts $s$ and $J(s)$---but large in terms of the paper's credibility and policy relevance.
