# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

This paper proposes that hedging demand against a negative AI singularity contributes to the high valuations of publicly traded AI stocks. The model is clean, the closed-form results are correct, and the decomposition into cash-flow and hedging components is a genuine contribution. The writing is engaging and the paper is well-structured. However, two issues weaken the paper's case for a top journal. First, the event study—the paper's only direct empirical test of the hedging channel—suffers from an identification problem that the paper does not adequately confront. Second, the paper does not sufficiently distinguish AI singularity risk from generic disaster risk, leaving a reader uncertain whether the hedging channel is specific to AI stocks or would apply to any asset that outperforms in recessions.

## Comments

1. **The event study does not cleanly identify singularity-risk shocks, and the paper should either sharpen identification or reframe what the evidence can show.** The six events in Table 4 (CAIS statement, UN session, Biden EO, Bletchley Summit, Grace et al. survey, EU AI Act) are presented as shocks to perceived singularity risk that are orthogonal to earnings expectations. But most of these events simultaneously signal that AI is economically important and that governments are taking AI seriously—information that revises long-term growth expectations upward, not just singularity probabilities. The paper's claim that "none of the six events coincided with earnings announcements or analyst forecast revisions" is too narrow: absence of concurrent analyst revisions does not mean the events are uninformative about future cash flows. A UN Security Council session on AI or a major executive order signals that AI will reshape the economy—this is bullish for AI growth expectations, not just for perceived tail risk. The CAIS extinction-risk statement is the cleanest event (it is hard to interpret a statement about human extinction as good news for AI earnings), but the others are confounded. The paper should either (a) develop a sharper identification strategy—for instance, restricting to events that are unambiguously bad news for AI (safety incidents, capability failures, alignment near-misses) yet still generate positive AI abnormal returns, which would be a smoking gun for the hedging channel; or (b) be more transparent about the confound, present the event study as suggestive rather than as a "preliminary test," and discuss what evidence *would* distinguish the hedging channel from a growth-expectations channel. As written, a skeptical reader can attribute all six positive CARs to growth-news and dismiss the hedging interpretation entirely.

2. **The paper needs to more sharply distinguish AI singularity risk from generic macroeconomic disaster risk, and explain why the hedging premium accrues specifically to AI stocks rather than to any asset with defensive properties.** The baseline singularity produces a 19% consumption drop (J ≈ 0.81)—comparable to a severe recession or a Barro-style rare disaster. The paper correctly notes that its model differs from standard rare-disaster frameworks because the consumption drop is asymmetric across assets. But this distinction deserves more development. In the real world, a 19% consumption decline would trigger flight-to-quality flows into Treasuries, gold, and defensive equities—yet the model has only two assets, so AI stocks are mechanically the only available hedge. In a richer asset menu, would the hedging premium survive? The paper should discuss whether AI stocks are empirically unique hedges for singularity risk or whether other assets (e.g., put options on labor-intensive sectors, long-duration bonds, or diversified technology ETFs) would also command a hedging premium, diluting the AI-specific channel. Relatedly, the displacement in the model is permanent and one-shot, which is stronger than what most investors likely envision. If displacement is gradual (a sequence of small shocks rather than one large jump), the rare-disaster logic weakens because each individual shock produces a smaller marginal-utility surge. A brief discussion of how the hedging premium scales with the granularity of the singularity—one large jump versus many small steps—would strengthen the paper's economic contribution and help a reader understand when the mechanism is quantitatively important versus negligible.
