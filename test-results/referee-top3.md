# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" proposes that public AI stock valuations are inflated by hedging demand against a negative AI singularity under incomplete markets. The model is clean, the closed-form results are elegant, and the decomposition of the AI premium into a cash-flow component and a hedging amplifier is a genuine contribution. The paper passes both spec-compliance and theory-correctness tests, and the writing is sharp throughout. However, two issues limit the paper's impact at a top journal. First, the empirical content does not extend beyond a single motivating figure; the paper's claim that the hedging channel is "empirically distinguishable" and "in principle testable" is unsubstantiated by any actual test. Second, the market-incompleteness friction — the essential ingredient — is asserted rather than measured, leaving the policy-relevant parameter $\alpha$ and the welfare analysis disconnected from observable data. Addressing these two gaps would substantially strengthen the paper.

## Comments

1. **The testable-implications section (3.4) needs real empirical content, not just a promissory note.** The paper's most important claim — that the hedging channel is "empirically distinguishable" from a pure growth story — currently rests on a theoretical argument (the premium responds to $\lambda$-shocks orthogonal to earnings revisions) and zero empirical evidence. For a top finance journal, this is insufficient even for a theory paper. The authors identify a clean identification strategy — events that shift perceived singularity risk without revising near-term earnings (AI-safety policy announcements, expert-survey publications, executive orders) — but then "leave this empirical test to future work." This is precisely the test that would make the paper publishable. At minimum, the paper should provide a preliminary event study: take a handful of salient AI-risk events (e.g., the 2023 executive order on AI safety, publication of expert surveys like Grace et al. 2024, major lab safety announcements) and examine whether AI stock valuations respond abnormally relative to non-AI stocks, controlling for contemporaneous earnings news. Even a small-sample descriptive analysis showing that AI stocks move on singularity-risk news would give the hedging channel empirical traction that it currently lacks. The cross-sectional prediction also deserves attention: within AI stocks, firms with higher exposure to the singularity scenario (pure-play AI vs. diversified tech) should command larger hedging premiums. The paper has the theory to generate these predictions; it needs to confront them with data. Without any empirical test, the hedging channel remains an unfalsified (and unfalsifiable) theoretical possibility, which is a hard sell at JF/JFE/RFS.

2. **The market-incompleteness friction needs to be grounded in measurable quantities, and the welfare analysis should connect to observable trends in market access.** The incomplete-markets friction is the mechanism that separates this paper from a standard disaster-risk model, yet its empirical magnitude is never estimated. The parameter $\alpha$ (market access) is the lever for the entire policy discussion and welfare analysis, but the paper does not say what $\alpha$ is today, how it has changed over time, or how it could be measured. This matters because the real-world landscape of private AI capital access is evolving rapidly: secondary markets for pre-IPO shares (e.g., Forge, EquityZen), AI-focused ETFs, sovereign wealth fund allocations, and regulatory changes (e.g., proposed expansions of the accredited investor definition) are all plausibly increasing $\alpha$. The paper should (a) discuss what observable proxies for $\alpha$ exist — e.g., the ratio of public to total AI market capitalization, household participation in venture/PE funds, the volume of secondary-market trading in AI shares — and (b) calibrate $\psi$ (the size of private AI capital relative to household consumption) from data rather than assuming $\psi = 0.15$. The current welfare result ($\omega$ up to 3.4%) is compelling but abstract; tying it to a measured $\alpha$ trajectory would transform it from a theoretical bound into a policy-relevant estimate. This would also sharpen the GKP comparison: the paper argues that its friction is "reducible in principle" unlike GKP's intergenerational friction, but without evidence that $\alpha$ is actually changing, this distinction is theoretical rather than practical.
