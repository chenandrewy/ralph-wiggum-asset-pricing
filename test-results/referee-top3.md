# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" proposes that incomplete-markets hedging demand contributes to elevated AI stock valuations. The model is clean, the core results (Propositions 1--4) are correctly derived, and the decomposition of the AI premium into cash-flow and hedging-amplification components is a genuine contribution. The calibration is transparent and the policy discussion around the market-access parameter is well-motivated. However, two issues limit the paper's publishability at a top journal. First, the assumption that AI owners are entirely absent from public equity markets is load-bearing but empirically fragile, and the paper does not adequately defend or stress-test it. Second, the model generates an important prediction about non-AI stock valuations that the paper never discusses, leaving the economic narrative incomplete. Addressing these two issues would significantly strengthen the contribution.

## Comments

1. **The assumption that AI owners do not participate in public equity markets needs stronger defense and a robustness analysis.** The entire hedging premium is driven by the representative household being the sole marginal investor in public AI stocks. The household's SDF has high marginal utility in the singularity state ($J < 1$), which generates the hedging amplifier $J^{-\gamma}$. But in practice, the largest shareholders of public AI companies are precisely the AI insiders the model assumes away — founders, early employees, and venture capitalists who took firms public. These agents *benefit* from the singularity, so their SDF would assign *low* marginal utility to the singularity state. If AI owners are even partially marginal in public AI equities, the effective SDF is a weighted average of household and AI-owner marginal utilities, and the hedging premium shrinks — potentially by a lot. The paper should (a) explicitly acknowledge this tension, (b) discuss why the representative-household-as-sole-marginal-investor assumption is a reasonable approximation (e.g., argue that insiders are constrained sellers who set quantities, not prices, or that their holdings are small relative to total public float after lockup expiration), and (c) ideally extend the model with a simple parametric robustness check — for instance, let a fraction $\eta$ of public AI shares be held by AI owners who are marginal investors, and show how the hedging premium degrades in $\eta$. This would parallel the $\alpha$ exercise for private-market access and would directly address the most natural objection a referee will raise.

2. **The model predicts that singularity risk raises non-AI stock valuations — discuss this explicitly and use it to sharpen the testable implications.** Table 1 shows that non-AI P/D ratios rise from 11.9 ($\lambda = 0$) to 13.2 ($\lambda = 0.05$), a 10% increase. This is counterintuitive: singularity risk *devastates* non-AI dividends ($\phi = 0.30$), yet non-AI stocks become *more* expensive. The mechanism is clear from equation (12) — $J^{-\gamma}(1-\phi) > 1$ at baseline, so the marginal-utility surge dominates the dividend drop — but the paper never mentions it. This omission matters for three reasons. First, it is a substantive, testable, cross-sectional prediction: the model says that *all* stocks should appreciate when perceived singularity risk rises, just AI stocks more so. If non-AI valuations do not co-move with singularity-risk proxies, the model faces a problem. Second, it affects the interpretation of the AI "premium" — both numerator and denominator of $v^A / v^N$ are moving, so the ratio understates the absolute valuation effect of singularity risk on AI stocks. Third, it connects to the rare-disaster literature in a novel way: unlike standard disaster models (Barro, Rietz) where disaster risk depresses all stock valuations, here the one-shot structure plus the asymmetric payoff means disaster risk raises valuations — a distinction worth highlighting. The paper should add a paragraph in Section 3.2 or 3.4 discussing the level effects on both stock types, note the conditions under which $J^{-\gamma}(1-\phi) < 1$ (so that non-AI stocks *fall* with singularity risk), and incorporate this as an additional testable implication alongside the existing discussion of Figure 2.
