# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" proposes that hedging demand against a negative AI singularity contributes to high AI stock valuations, with incomplete markets amplifying the premium. The model is clean, the closed-form results are elegant, and the decomposition into cash-flow and hedging components is a genuine contribution. The extension incorporating extinction risk and heterogeneous beliefs is well-integrated. However, the paper's headline quantitative claim---that differential pre-singularity growth matches the observed 2--2.7x AI valuation gap---is presented only in prose without a supporting exhibit, making it unverifiable. More fundamentally, the paper derives price-dividend ratios but never discusses the expected return implications of the hedging channel, which is a significant omission for a finance audience. A hedging/insurance asset should command *low* expected returns, yet AI stocks have delivered high realized returns; this tension must be addressed.

## Comments

1. **The differential-growth calibration needs a proper exhibit.** The paper's most important quantitative result---that with 3--5 pp growth differentials the model generates P/D ratios of 2.0--2.7x, matching the data---appears only in a single prose paragraph (Section 3.3, "Differential Pre-Singularity Growth"). No table or figure supports this claim. The reader sees "approximately 2.0 to 2.7" but cannot verify the numbers or examine the decomposition into hedging vs. growth components under differential growth. The paper uses only 5 of its 6 allowed exhibits. This is the result that closes the gap between the model and the data; it deserves at least a table showing $v^A/v^N$ for a grid of $(g^A - g^N, \lambda)$ values, with columns decomposing the premium into its hedging-amplifier and cash-flow components. Without this, the paper's central empirical claim rests on an unverifiable assertion.

2. **The paper should derive and discuss expected returns, not just P/D ratios.** The model makes a sharp, testable prediction about expected returns that the paper never states. Because AI stocks pay well when the SDF is high (the singularity state), they are insurance assets: $\text{Cov}(M, R^A) > 0$, which implies AI stocks should earn a *lower* risk premium than non-AI stocks. This is the flip side of the high P/D ratio---expensive insurance assets have low expected returns. The paper should (a) derive the expected return differential in closed form (which is straightforward given the existing setup), and (b) discuss the tension with data: AI stocks have delivered *high* realized returns over the sample period. This tension is not fatal---high realized returns on an insurance asset are consistent with the singularity not having occurred yet, plus upward revisions in perceived singularity risk---but the paper must confront it explicitly. A top finance journal audience will immediately notice that the hedging story implies low expected returns, and silence on this point will be read as an oversight rather than an omission.
