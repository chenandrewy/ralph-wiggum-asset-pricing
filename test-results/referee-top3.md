# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" proposes that public AI stocks command a valuation premium because they hedge against a negative AI singularity under incomplete markets. The model is clean, the closed-form results are correct, and the decomposition into cash-flow and hedging components is a genuine contribution. The paper is well-written and concise for a theory piece. However, two issues weaken the manuscript. First, the event study (Table 4) cannot identify the hedging channel—as the paper itself concedes—and consumes one of only six exhibit slots on evidence that is at best ambiguous. Replacing it with a figure that illustrates the model mechanism (e.g., the premium decomposition across parameters, or the hump shape from Proposition 6) would far better serve a theory paper. Second, at 22 pages the paper exceeds its own 20-page target; the defensive digressions in Section 3.3 ("Why AI Stocks, Not Treasuries or Gold?" and the gradual-singularity discussion) are the natural candidates for cuts, as they anticipate objections without advancing the core argument.

## Comments

1. **Replace the event study (Table 4) with a model-mechanism figure.** The event study is the weakest element of the paper. Six events, no formal statistical tests (no t-statistics, no bootstrapped confidence intervals), and—most critically—the paper itself explains at length exactly why these events cannot distinguish the hedging channel from a growth-expectations story (paragraph beginning "We emphasize that this evidence is suggestive, not dispositive"). Including an exhibit that the authors must immediately discredit undermines rather than supports the contribution. A theory paper's exhibits should illuminate the mechanism. A figure showing how the valuation premium decomposes into its cash-flow and hedging components across the key parameter space—say, $\lambda \times (g^A - g^N)$ as in Table 2, or the hump shape from Proposition 6—would give readers visual intuition for the novel mechanism and make the paper's core contribution more memorable. This also resolves a presentational imbalance: the paper currently has five tables and one data figure, but no figure illustrating any model output. A second benefit: removing the event-study discussion (Section 3.4's empirical paragraphs and Table 4) would recover roughly two pages, helping with the length issue below.

2. **Cut the paper to 20 pages by removing defensive digressions in Section 3.3.** The compiled paper runs to 22 pages (including references), exceeding the 20-page spec. The most productive cuts are the two extended defensive paragraphs in Section 3.3: "Why AI Stocks, Not Treasuries or Gold?" (~1 page) and the gradual-singularity discussion (~0.5 page). Both anticipate reasonable objections, but do so at length that is disproportionate to their value. The Treasuries/gold discussion can be condensed to two sentences: the singularity is an asymmetric shock, so only assets with positive AI exposure can hedge it; safe-haven assets are orthogonal to AI-specific displacement. The gradual-singularity point is already made by the self-limiting mechanism in the model and does not need a separate discussion. Similarly, "Measuring Market Access and Private AI Capital" (Section 3.3) runs long on observable proxies for $\alpha$ and $\psi$; the key data points could be folded into the calibration discussion in a single paragraph. These cuts, combined with the event-study removal from Comment 1, would comfortably bring the paper within the 20-page limit while sharpening its focus on the theoretical contribution.
