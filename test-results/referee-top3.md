# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" proposes that hedging demand against a negative AI singularity contributes to elevated AI stock valuations. The model is clean, the closed-form results are correct, and the decomposition into cash-flow and hedging-amplification components is a genuine contribution. The paper is well-written, concise, and the GKP differentiation is clearly articulated. However, two substantive gaps weaken the paper's case for a top journal. First, the paper's empirical engagement is limited to a single motivating figure; none of the model's distinguishing comparative statics are tested, so the hedging channel remains observationally indistinguishable from a pure growth-expectations story. Second, the microfoundation of friction resolution in Section 4.3 is disconnected from the economic forces that sustain the friction in Section 2.3, undermining the paper's most novel claim—that, unlike GKP, this friction is reducible. Addressing these two issues would substantially strengthen the contribution.

## Comments

1. **The hedging channel lacks a distinguishing empirical test.** The paper's core claim is that hedging demand—not just growth expectations—drives AI valuations. But the only empirical content is Figure 1, which documents the AI valuation premium without distinguishing between the two channels. The back-of-envelope decomposition on page 10 is suggestive but not a test: it attributes the residual to growth differentials by assumption, rather than by measurement. A top-journal referee will ask: what observable pattern would be present if the hedging channel operates and absent if only growth expectations drive the premium? The model delivers sharp comparative statics—the premium increases in λ (singularity probability) and decreases in s (AI portfolio share)—that are, in principle, testable. For example, the hedging channel predicts that the AI premium should spike when measures of perceived singularity risk increase (e.g., expert survey updates, major AI capability announcements), controlling for revisions to expected earnings. It also predicts that as the AI share of public equity rises (s increases), the premium should erode—a self-limiting mechanism with time-series implications. Even a reduced-form event study or a time-series correlation between the AI premium and a proxy for λ would move the paper from "consistent with" to "evidence for." The paper is 13 pages with 4 exhibits, well under the 20-page and 6-exhibit limits; there is room to add one empirical exhibit that disciplines the mechanism. Without such a test, a reader can fully rationalize Figure 1 with differential growth expectations alone, and the hedging channel, however elegant, remains a theoretical possibility rather than a demonstrated force.

2. **The microfoundation of friction resolution (Section 4.3) is disconnected from the friction's economic rationale (Section 2.3).** Section 2.3 offers three compelling reasons why the incomplete-markets friction persists: information asymmetry in valuing proprietary AI technology, control and incentive motives for retaining private ownership, and regulatory barriers to household access. These are the forces that distinguish the paper's friction from GKP's intergenerational one and that justify calling it "reducible." But when Section 4.3 microfounds the friction-resolution probability π, it ignores all three forces and instead introduces a generic fixed cost k that AI owners pay to share capital. The result—π(Y_O) = 1 − k/Y_O—is mechanically clean but economically thin. Why is the cost fixed rather than proportional to the value being shared? How does infinite output resolve information asymmetry (the outsider still cannot evaluate proprietary algorithms) or the control motive (the AI owner still loses governance rights)? A more satisfying microfoundation would tie π explicitly to at least one of the three stated forces—for instance, showing that when Y_O is large, the adverse-selection discount becomes negligible relative to the gains from trade, or that the control motive weakens because the marginal value of governance declines in output scale. This would close the gap between the verbal economics in Section 2.3 and the formal model in Section 4.3, and it would sharpen the policy interpretation of α: the paper could say which specific policy lever (securitization standards, accredited-investor reform, IPO incentives) maps to which force, rather than treating α as an undifferentiated parameter. The current disconnect risks the criticism that the "reducible friction" claim is asserted rather than demonstrated within the model.
