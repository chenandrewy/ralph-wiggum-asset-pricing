# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" presents a clean consumption-based model in which public AI stocks command a valuation premium because they partially hedge a negative AI singularity under incomplete markets. The decomposition into a cash-flow premium and a hedging amplifier is elegant, the closed-form results are correct, and the extension incorporating extinction risk and friction resolution is well-motivated. The paper is concise, well-written, and tells a unified story. However, the model's most interesting dimension---the evolution of perceived singularity risk---is frozen by the constant-$\lambda$ assumption, leaving the theory unable to speak to the very time-series dynamics (the sharp post-2023 widening of AI premia) that Figure 1 uses to motivate the paper. Additionally, the testable-implications section identifies a clean identifying prediction but stops short of attempting even preliminary empirical corroboration, which weakens the paper's claim that the hedging channel is a "qualitatively different force" with "testable implications tied to perceived singularity risk."

## Comments

1. **The constant-$\lambda$ assumption creates a tension between the model and its motivating evidence.** Figure 1 shows a dramatic widening of the AI valuation premium after 2023, which the introduction implicitly attributes to rising awareness of singularity risk (the emergence of large language models). But the model assumes $\lambda$ is fixed. With constant $\lambda$ and common growth, the premium is time-invariant. With differential growth ($g^A > g^N$), the AI share $s_t$ rises over time, which by Proposition 2(ii) *erodes* the premium---the opposite of what Figure 1 shows. The model thus cannot explain the central stylized fact it cites. This is a first-order issue for a paper that positions hedging demand as "part of the explanation" for high AI valuations. The authors should either (a) introduce time-varying beliefs about singularity risk---even a simple two-state Markov chain for $\lambda_t$ would suffice---and show that belief shocks can generate the observed dynamics, or (b) explicitly acknowledge this limitation and reframe the contribution as a cross-sectional story (why AI stocks are expensive *relative to non-AI stocks* at any point in time) rather than a time-series story (why the gap is widening). Option (a) would also provide a natural framework for the event-study identification the paper acknowledges is needed: shocks to $\lambda_t$ are the objects that would be identified in the data, and the model currently has no language for them.

2. **The testable-implications section should attempt at least one reduced-form test rather than deferring all empirical work.** The paper identifies a sharp prediction: the AI premium should respond to perceived singularity risk *conditional on* earnings expectations. This is the key distinguishing feature of the hedging channel relative to a pure growth story. But Section 3.4 describes this as "an open empirical challenge" and stops there. For a top finance journal, even a theory paper benefits enormously from demonstrating that its mechanism has a detectable empirical footprint. A natural exercise---feasible with existing data---would be an event study around AI capability or safety announcements (e.g., GPT-4 release, major AI safety incidents, executive orders on AI regulation), examining whether AI stocks exhibit abnormal returns relative to earnings-revision controls. The prediction is specific: events that shift perceived singularity risk without revising near-term earnings should move AI valuations through the hedging amplifier. Even suggestive evidence (a table of 5--10 events with abnormal returns and confidence intervals) would substantially strengthen the paper's empirical relevance. Without it, the "testable implications" read more like a promissory note than a contribution.
