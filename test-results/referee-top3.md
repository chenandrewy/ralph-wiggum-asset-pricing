# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" presents a clean, well-executed consumption-based asset pricing model in which incomplete markets generate hedging demand for public AI stocks against a negative AI singularity. The theoretical contribution is sound: the decomposition of the AI valuation premium into a cash-flow component and a hedging amplifier (equation 14) is elegant, and the extensions incorporating extinction risk and heterogeneous beliefs are well-motivated by Jones (2024). Both the spec-compliance and theory-correctness tests pass. The paper's main vulnerability is that its two central modeling choices --- the calibration of singularity parameters and the exogeneity of market incompleteness --- lack empirical discipline, which weakens the paper's ability to make quantitative claims about observed AI valuations. Addressing these two issues would substantially strengthen the paper's contribution.

## Comments

1. **The singularity parameters ($\lambda$, $\theta$, $\phi$) lack empirical grounding.** The calibration exercises in Tables 1--2 vary these parameters freely but provide no external evidence to anchor them. For a paper aimed at a top finance journal, this is a significant gap. The rare disaster literature the paper cites (Barro 2006) disciplines disaster probabilities using over a century of cross-country macroeconomic data. The AI singularity has no historical precedent, but there are credible external sources that could provide discipline: surveys of AI researchers on timelines and probability of transformative AI (e.g., Grace et al. 2024, "Thousands of AI Authors on the Future of AI"), forecasting platforms, and industry estimates of the private-vs-public split in AI capital. Similarly, $\theta = 0.50$ and $\phi = 0.30$ are described as "severe but not catastrophic," but the paper offers no evidence for why these magnitudes are plausible relative to historical technology shocks or expert assessments of AI impact on labor shares. Without empirical anchors, the calibration section reads as an illustration of the model's mechanics rather than a quantitative claim about real asset prices --- yet the paper repeatedly compares model output to the 2--2.7x ratio in the data (Section 3.3, Conclusion). Either ground the parameters in external evidence or soften the quantitative claims to be purely illustrative.

2. **Market incompleteness is assumed exogenously, creating a tension with the paper's own logic.** The entire hedging premium depends on $\alpha = 0$ (or near zero) --- the household cannot access private AI capital. But the paper's own calibration shows this friction generates a 29% valuation premium at baseline and potentially much more under alternative parameters. A premium of this magnitude implies large gains from trade: financial intermediaries have strong incentives to securitize private AI capital, private AI firms have incentives to IPO or issue tracking securities, and households have incentives to seek indirect exposure through venture funds or structured products. The paper should address why this friction persists in equilibrium despite the large rents it creates. Section 4.3 microfounds friction *resolution* conditional on singularity (via infinite output making sharing cheap), but this is a post-singularity story. The pre-singularity question is more pressing: if everyone knows the friction generates a 29% premium, why doesn't financial innovation erode it *before* the singularity? The GKP comparison actually highlights this problem --- GKP's friction (unborn future innovators) is structurally irreducible, whereas this paper's friction (private capital held by existing agents) is precisely the kind that financial markets specialize in resolving. A paragraph acknowledging this tension and discussing the forces that sustain the friction (e.g., information asymmetry about AI capabilities, regulatory barriers to securitizing AI assets, the option value of retaining control) would significantly strengthen the economic argument.
