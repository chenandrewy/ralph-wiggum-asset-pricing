# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" proposes that hedging demand against a negative AI singularity contributes to the high valuations of public AI stocks. The theoretical framework is clean, the closed-form results are elegant, and the extension incorporating extinction risk and friction resolution is well-motivated. The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by connecting AI valuation puzzles to incomplete markets and disaster risk in a tractable way. However, two issues weaken the paper's quantitative credibility and should be addressed before publication at a top journal: (1) the main calibration tables use a baseline market-access parameter of zero even though the paper itself estimates the real-world value at 0.60--0.70, creating an internal tension that overstates the headline numbers, and (2) the model's expected-return predictions are counterfactual and the proposed reconciliation via business-cycle augmentation is quantitatively inadequate, leaving the paper's pricing mechanism in tension with the return data.

## Comments

1. **The baseline calibration at $\alpha = 0$ is inconsistent with the paper's own estimate of real-world market access.** Section 3.3 ("Market Access in Practice") persuasively argues that $\alpha \approx 0.60$--$0.70$ today, based on the ratio of public to total AI market capitalization. Yet all headline results---Tables 1 and 2, and the key claim that the hedging channel contributes "13--23% of the valuation gap"---are computed at $\alpha = 0$, where the household has zero access to private AI capital. Table 3 shows that at $\alpha = 1.00$, the premium ratio falls from 1.30 to 1.22. Interpolating, the premium at $\alpha \approx 0.60$--$0.70$ is substantially below 1.30, and the hedging share of the differential-growth premium (Table 2) would shrink correspondingly. The paper's own data suggest the mechanism is quantitatively weaker than the headline numbers imply. This is not merely a presentational issue---it undermines the quantitative anchor of the paper's contribution. The fix is straightforward: either recalibrate the main tables at a realistic $\alpha$ (and recompute Table 2's hedging shares at that $\alpha$), or explain clearly why $\alpha = 0$ is the right thought experiment despite the real-world estimate. The welfare calculations also start from $\alpha = 0$, but if the household already has $\alpha \approx 0.65$, the marginal welfare gain from further broadening market access is much smaller than 3.4%. Presenting the welfare gain from, say, $\alpha = 0.65$ to $\alpha = 1.0$ would be more policy-relevant and more honest.

2. **The counterfactual expected-return prediction needs a more satisfying resolution.** The baseline model predicts that AI stocks earn *lower* expected returns than non-AI stocks (positive $\text{Cov}(M, R^A)$), which directly contradicts the data: AI stocks have higher realized returns, higher betas, and higher volatility. Proposition 3 augments the model with business-cycle risk, but the reconciliation is quantitatively hollow---the business-cycle premium differential is only 5--7 basis points, and matching observed return differentials requires $\sigma \approx 0.15$, which the paper acknowledges is a "standard limitation of consumption-based models." A top journal will ask: if the mechanism that is supposed to explain *valuations* simultaneously generates counterfactual *return* predictions, and the patch that fixes returns doesn't work at reasonable parameters, how confident should we be in the valuation explanation? The paper could strengthen this section in several ways: (a) provide a clearer quantitative decomposition showing that the hedging discount on expected returns is small relative to the business-cycle premium at realistic (not consumption-based) volatilities, so the prediction is "nearly zero" rather than "wrong sign"; (b) connect to the peso-problem interpretation more formally---if the singularity has not occurred, realized returns are mechanically higher, and this is a testable prediction about future returns conditional on singularity arrival; or (c) acknowledge that the model is a valuation model, not a return model, and sharpen the empirical predictions accordingly. As it stands, the business-cycle augmentation reads as an afterthought that concedes the point rather than resolving it.
