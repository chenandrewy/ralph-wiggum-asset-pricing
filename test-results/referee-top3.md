# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" proposes that public AI stocks command a valuation premium because they partially hedge against a negative AI singularity under incomplete markets. The model is clean, the closed-form results are correct, and the decomposition into cash-flow and hedging-amplifier components is elegant. The paper tells a unified story and is well-written. However, two issues weaken the contribution at the margin. First, the testable-implications section (Section 3.4) is the paper's softest point: it occupies substantial real estate but ultimately concedes that no test is executed, no clean identification strategy exists, and suitable proxies are unavailable---leaving the reader with suggestive time-series plots and a promissory note for future work. Second, at the paper's own empirically relevant calibrations (3--5 pp growth differential, moderate $\lambda$), the hedging channel contributes only 13--23% of the premium, which raises a natural question about the economic magnitude of the novel mechanism relative to the well-understood growth channel. Addressing these two issues would substantially strengthen the paper's case for publication at a top journal.

## Comments

1. **The testable-implications section needs either a sharper empirical strategy or significant compression.** Section 3.4 currently runs roughly two pages (plus Figure 2) and is the most defensive part of the paper. It proposes a test---regress the AI premium on a singularity-risk proxy controlling for earnings revisions---then immediately concedes that (a) the model treats $\lambda$ as fixed rather than as a state variable, (b) suitable proxies for perceived singularity risk "are not yet well-established," and (c) disentangling singularity-risk revisions from correlated earnings revisions "is beyond the scope of this theoretical contribution." A referee at JF/JFE/RFS will read this as the authors identifying their own paper's main vulnerability and then declining to address it. Two paths would improve the paper. *Option A*: Propose a concrete, feasible identification strategy---even if unexecuted---that goes beyond the generic "regress on a proxy" suggestion. For example, the model predicts that the AI premium responds to singularity risk *conditional on* earnings expectations (Proposition 2(i)). Events that plausibly shift perceived singularity risk without simultaneously revising near-term AI earnings---such as government AI-safety policy announcements, or expert-survey publications like the ones cited (Grace et al.)---could serve as instruments or event-study shocks. Spelling out such a strategy in concrete terms (what is the dependent variable, what is the proxy, what is the control, what is the predicted sign) would give the section real teeth without requiring the authors to run the test. *Option B*: If no clean strategy can be articulated, compress the section to a single paragraph acknowledging the testability limitation, cut Figure 2 (which is suggestive but not dispositive), and reallocate the freed space to strengthening other parts of the paper (e.g., the policy/welfare discussion or the incomplete-markets microfoundation). The current middle ground---extensive discussion of a test the paper cannot execute---is the least effective option.

2. **Sharpen the paper's value proposition given the modest quantitative contribution of the hedging channel at empirical calibrations.** At the calibrations the paper itself calls "empirically relevant" (Table 2: $g^A - g^N$ of 3--5 pp, $\lambda = 0.02$--$0.05$), the hedging amplifier accounts for 13--23% of the AI premium. The remaining 77--87% is the differential-growth channel, which is conceptually straightforward and does not require the incomplete-markets apparatus. The paper addresses this honestly, and notes that under more severe calibrations ($\gamma = 5$, $\phi_L = 0.35$) the hedging share can exceed 50%. But a skeptical referee will ask: if the novel mechanism is second-order at baseline, why build a paper around it? The authors should make a stronger affirmative case for why the 13--23% hedging share matters. Several angles are available but underexploited: (a) *The hedging channel is the only component that depends on incomplete markets*, so it is the part of the premium that policy can directly affect---the welfare gains from broadening market access ($\omega$ up to 3.4%) flow entirely through this channel. Connecting the quantitative policy relevance more tightly to the hedging share would strengthen the motivation. (b) *The hedging channel generates qualitatively different cross-sectional and time-series predictions* (response to singularity-risk shocks rather than earnings revisions), making it empirically distinguishable even if quantitatively modest---an asset-pricing theory paper's contribution can rest on identifying a new mechanism rather than on matching magnitudes. (c) *The 13--23% range is a lower bound* given conservative calibration of $\phi_L$; the paper could discuss what labor-displacement evidence (e.g., from the rapid adoption of LLMs in white-collar tasks since 2023) would push $\phi_L$ toward the higher values where the hedging channel dominates. Making one or more of these arguments explicitly---rather than leaving the reader to infer them---would preempt the "so what?" objection and clarify the paper's contribution.
