# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" proposes that public AI stocks command a valuation premium because they hedge against a negative AI singularity under incomplete markets. The model is clean, the closed-form results are correct (verified by tests), and the decomposition into cash-flow and hedging-amplifier components is elegant. The paper meets its spec requirements and both automated tests pass. However, the paper has a significant gap between its theoretical ambition and its empirical credibility that, if addressed, would substantially strengthen its case for a top journal. The two issues below focus on where the paper falls short of the spec's goals—particularly the requirement that "the narrative is consistent with the results" and that the paper tells "a clear, unified, and concise story"—in ways the existing tests did not catch.

## Comments

1. **The event-study evidence is too weak to include and risks undermining the paper's credibility.** The spec requires testable implications tied to singularity risk (Section I.3), and the paper delivers these theoretically (Section 3.4). But the event-study table (Table 4) attempts to validate the first prediction with only six hand-picked events, no statistical tests, no controls for contemporaneous earnings news, and no formal identification strategy. The authors acknowledge this ("suggestive, not definitive"), yet the table occupies a full exhibit slot (one of six allowed) and a substantial discussion. At a top journal, this will draw immediate fire: referees will ask why, if these events are exogenous singularity-risk shocks, the Biden Executive Order—arguably the most salient AI-risk policy event in the sample—goes the wrong way. The paper would be stronger if it either (a) dropped Table 4 and the event-study discussion entirely, reallocating the exhibit slot and page space to strengthening other parts of the paper, or (b) committed to a rigorous event study with a larger sample, formal hypothesis tests, and controls for simultaneous earnings-relevant information. Half-measures—presenting informal evidence while disclaiming its limitations—satisfy neither theorists nor empiricists. The theoretical predictions in Section 3.4 are sharp and stand on their own; the informal event study weakens rather than strengthens them by inviting methodological objections that distract from the model's contribution.

2. **The paper does not adequately develop how the model's incomplete-markets friction maps to observable, time-varying quantities, which limits its policy relevance.** The spec emphasizes that "financial market solutions to AI disaster risk are under-discussed" (I.3) and that broadening market access ($\alpha$) is a central policy lever. The paper derives clean comparative statics in $\alpha$ (Corollary 1, welfare gains up to 3.4%), but never seriously discusses what $\alpha$ is in practice, how large $\psi$ is, or how one would measure progress along the $\alpha$-trajectory. Line 135 mentions "public-to-total AI market capitalization and secondary-market trading volume" as proxies but does not pursue them. This matters for two reasons. First, the policy claim—that securitization and regulatory reform yield welfare gains—is only as convincing as the mapping from $\alpha$ to real-world instruments; without this, the welfare numbers are illustrative but not actionable. Second, the distinction from GKP (whose friction is "structurally irreducible") is one of the paper's main contributions, but it remains purely theoretical without evidence that $\alpha$ has moved or plausible estimates of where it sits today. A short discussion—perhaps replacing the event-study space—quantifying the current $\alpha$ (e.g., ratio of public AI market cap to total AI-sector value including private firms), noting its trend, and connecting the welfare estimates to specific policy proposals (expanding accredited-investor rules, creating AI-sector index funds with private-capital exposure) would make the incomplete-markets story concrete and the policy implications actionable. This would also more fully satisfy the spec's requirement that the paper explains how its friction differs from GKP's in a substantively useful way, not just a formal one.
