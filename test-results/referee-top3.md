# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" proposes that hedging demand against a negative AI singularity contributes to elevated AI stock valuations. The model is clean, the closed-form results are elegant, and the decomposition of the AI premium into a cash-flow component and a hedging amplifier is a genuinely useful conceptual contribution. The paper successfully formalizes the incomplete-markets intuition: households excluded from private AI capital overpay for public AI stocks because those stocks pay well in the state where marginal utility is highest. The extension incorporating extinction risk, heterogeneous beliefs, and endogenous friction resolution is well-integrated and connects naturally to Jones (2024). However, two issues limit the paper's persuasiveness for a top-journal audience. First, the quantitative contribution of the hedging channel is modest at the calibrations needed to match the data, which creates tension with the paper's framing. Second, the testable implications section describes an empirical strategy but does not execute it, leaving the paper's central identifying claim—that the hedging channel is "independently testable" and "qualitatively different" from growth expectations—unsubstantiated.

## Comments

1. **The hedging channel is quantitatively second-order at empirically relevant calibrations, and the paper needs to confront this tension more directly.** At the calibrations required to approach the observed 2--2.7x AI-to-market valuation ratio (Table 2), the hedging amplifier accounts for only 13--23% of the combined premium; differential pre-singularity growth does the rest. The paper's framing—title, abstract, introduction—foregrounds the hedging channel as the main contribution, but the calibration tells a story in which growth differentials are the dominant force and hedging is a supplement. This is not a fatal problem—a 13--23% contribution from a novel, independently identifiable channel is economically meaningful—but the paper should own this result more squarely rather than toggling between "the hedging channel alone generates a 30% premium" (Table 1, common growth rates) and the differential-growth calibrations where it is a minority contributor. The severe-calibration rows in Table 3 ($\gamma = 5$, $\phi_L = 0.35$) do make the hedging channel first-order, but these require jointly high risk aversion and high labor displacement. I would suggest: (a) restructure the calibration narrative so the baseline presentation is the differential-growth model (Table 2), since this is the empirically relevant case, and frame the common-growth-rate results (Table 1) as an illustrative special case that isolates the mechanism; (b) discuss explicitly what combination of parameters makes the hedging channel quantitatively dominant and assess whether those parameters are empirically defensible; and (c) consider whether additional sources of incompleteness—such as uninsurable idiosyncratic labor-income risk upon singularity—could amplify the hedging channel without requiring extreme parameters.

2. **The paper claims the hedging channel generates "independently testable" predictions but provides no formal test, which is a significant gap for a top finance journal.** Section 3.4 articulates a clean identifying restriction: the AI valuation premium should respond to perceived singularity risk even after controlling for earnings expectations. The paper then describes a regression of the AI premium on a singularity-risk proxy, controlling for consensus earnings revisions—but does not run it. For a theory paper at the JF/JFE/RFS level, it is not strictly necessary to provide a full empirical analysis, but when the paper explicitly frames the hedging channel as "identifiable because it responds to perceived singularity risk, not earnings forecasts" (Introduction), and when the testable-implications section proposes specific tests, the absence of even a reduced-form event study is conspicuous. Figure 2 shows that AI-to-market valuation ratios spike at AI capability milestones, which is suggestive, but this pattern is equally consistent with upward revisions to expected AI earnings—exactly the confound the paper identifies. At minimum, the paper should: (a) run the proposed regression (or a close variant) using a proxy for perceived singularity risk (e.g., AI capability announcement intensity, expert survey updates, or media-based measures of AI existential concern) and show that it predicts the AI premium after controlling for consensus earnings revisions; or (b) if the data do not currently permit a clean test, be transparent about this limitation and downgrade the "independently testable" language to "in principle testable" with a concrete discussion of what data would be needed. Delivering even a simple, imperfect empirical test would move the paper from a theoretical possibility result to a paper with bite, and would substantially strengthen the case for publication at a top journal.
