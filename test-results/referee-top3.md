# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" is a well-crafted paper with a clean model, a genuinely novel economic mechanism, and crisp analytical results. The core idea---that incomplete markets make public AI stocks a hedge against a negative AI singularity, generating a valuation premium---is timely, important, and clearly communicated. The formal propositions are correct and the calibration is transparent. However, two issues weaken the paper relative to its specification and the standard expected at a top finance journal. First, the paper runs to 22 pages, exceeding its stated 20-page maximum; tightening is needed. Second, the business-cycle augmentation in Section 3.4---which is the paper's only answer to the glaring empirical challenge that AI stocks have high betas and high expected returns---is presented informally, without a proposition or proof, making it feel like an afterthought rather than an integrated part of the model. These two issues are connected: formalizing the business-cycle result would require space that must be found by cutting elsewhere.

## Comments

1. **The paper exceeds the 20-page limit (22 pages vs. 20 max).** The specification requires at most 20 pages. The compiled PDF runs to 22 pages (the references alone consume nearly two full pages). The automated spec-compliance test reported 13 pages and passed, but the page images clearly show 22 numbered pages. This is a binding constraint. The paper should be tightened to fit. Candidates for compression include: (a) the proof of Proposition 1, which is a standard Euler-equation manipulation that could be shortened or moved to an appendix if one is created; (b) the inline $\eta$-robustness discussion in Section 2.3 (currently ~10 lines of text plus numerical examples), which could be condensed to a single sentence noting the robustness check and pointing to Table 3; (c) the "Level Effects on Non-AI Stocks" paragraph, which is interesting but tangential to the main argument and could be shortened to two sentences. Together these changes would recover approximately 1.5--2 pages without sacrificing any results.

2. **The business-cycle augmentation needs to be formalized.** The expected-returns discussion in Section 3.4 is the paper's answer to its most obvious empirical vulnerability: in the baseline model, AI stocks earn *lower* expected returns than non-AI stocks (a hedging discount), yet in the data they have high betas, high volatility, and high realized returns. The paper resolves this by adding i.i.d. business-cycle shocks with heterogeneous loadings ($b^A > b^N$), showing that the cyclical premium can dominate the hedging discount. But this argument is presented entirely as prose and back-of-envelope arithmetic ("$b^A \approx 1.3$--$1.5$", "approximately 2--3 percentage points", "approximately 80 basis points"). Unlike every other result in the paper, it has no proposition, no proof, and no table. A top-journal referee will notice this asymmetry and ask: Does the joint equilibrium with both risks actually exist under the same parameter restrictions ($a < 1$, etc.)? Does adding business-cycle risk change the hedging premium in Proposition 2, or is the decomposition in equation (17) exact? The fix is to state a brief proposition establishing that, with i.i.d. normal-period shocks, the P/D expressions in Propositions 1--2 are unchanged to first order in $\sigma^2$ and the expected-return decomposition (17) is exact conditional on no singularity. This would cost roughly half a page---space that can be found by implementing the cuts recommended in Comment 1.
