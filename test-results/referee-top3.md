# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

"Hedging the Singularity" proposes that hedging demand against a negative AI singularity contributes to the high valuations of public AI stocks. The model is clean, the closed-form results are elegant, and the economic mechanism is novel and well-motivated. The paper reads well and the extension incorporating extinction risk and heterogeneous beliefs is a natural complement. However, two issues weaken the paper's contribution: (1) the quantitative calibration compares model-implied price-dividend ratios to empirical price-earnings ratios without reconciling the two, undermining the paper's main empirical claim; and (2) the infinite-output discussion in Section 4.3 is informal and underdeveloped relative to the paper's own ambitions, leaving the most provocative theoretical insight—that the premium is largest in an intermediate regime—without formal support.

## Comments

1. **The calibration compares price-dividend ratios to price-earnings ratios.** The model derives closed-form P/D ratios, and the calibration in Table 1 reports these. But Figure 1 plots trailing P/E ratios, and the text claims that the model's 29% premium at $\lambda = 0.02$ is "broadly consistent with the elevated valuations in Figure 1." P/D and P/E are different objects: $P/E = (P/D) \times (D/E)$, and the payout ratio $D/E$ varies enormously across firms. Major AI companies retain most of their earnings and many pay no dividends at all, so their P/D ratios would be far higher than their P/E ratios. The paper should either (a) recast Figure 1 using empirical P/D ratios so the comparison is apples-to-apples, (b) map the model's P/D ratios into P/E equivalents using reasonable payout assumptions, or (c) at minimum acknowledge the gap and explain why the comparison remains informative despite this difference. As it stands, the paper's central quantitative claim—that a 2–5% singularity probability accounts for "a substantial share" of the AI premium—lacks a valid empirical benchmark.

2. **Section 4.3 (Infinite Output and Market Completeness) needs formal development.** The paper's most interesting theoretical observation is that the hedging premium is largest in an intermediate regime: the singularity must be harmful enough to justify insurance but not so extreme as to trivially resolve the incomplete-markets friction. Section 4.3 makes this argument in two informal paragraphs, referencing $\pi \to 1$ from Proposition 4 as the limiting case. But $\pi$ is introduced in Proposition 4 as an exogenous friction-resolution probability that is "increasing in $\delta_O$"—it is not derived from the economics of infinite output or the cost of sharing resources. The paper should formalize this channel: for instance, a proposition showing how the premium behaves as the scale of post-singularity AI output grows without bound, with $\pi$ microfounded as a function of the output level. This would complete the paper's engagement with Jones (2024), sharpen the contrast with GKP's permanent intergenerational friction, and give the "paradoxical" intermediate-regime result the formal standing it deserves. Without this, Section 4.3 reads as a promissory note rather than a delivered result.
