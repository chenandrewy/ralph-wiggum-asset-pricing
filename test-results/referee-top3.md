# referee-top3
REVIEW — 2026-03-22

## Summary

This paper proposes that hedging demand against a negative AI singularity contributes to elevated AI stock valuations, and that incomplete markets—exclusion from private AI capital—amplify this premium. The model is clean, the closed-form results are elegant, and the decomposition into cash-flow and hedging components is a genuine contribution. Both the theory and the spec compliance are solid. However, two substantive issues limit the paper's readiness for a top journal. First, the quantitative importance of the hedging channel is overstated relative to what the calibration actually delivers: once differential growth is introduced to match the data, the hedging share shrinks to 13–23%, yet the narrative frames hedging as the headline mechanism. Second, the testable-implications section appeals to time-varying perceived singularity risk to interpret the data (Figure 2), but the model itself has constant $\lambda$—there is no learning, no belief updating, and no mechanism for $\lambda$ to move. This creates a gap between the model and the empirical patterns it claims to explain.

## Comments

1. **The narrative overstates the quantitative role of the hedging channel relative to growth expectations.** At baseline ($g^A = g^N$), the hedging channel generates a 30% premium (Table 1), which is a meaningful but incomplete contribution toward the observed 2–2.7$\times$ gap. To approach the data, the paper introduces differential pre-singularity growth of 3–5 percentage points (Table 2), which brings the ratio to 1.8–2.3$\times$. But at these calibrations, the hedging share—reported in parentheses—is only 13–23%. The growth differential is doing most of the work, and the growth differential is not a novel mechanism (it is standard Gordon growth logic). The introduction claims that "incomplete markets roughly double the premium relative to expected cash flows alone," which is true at baseline but misleading in the calibrations that actually match the data. The paper should reconcile the narrative with the numbers more carefully. One constructive path: reframe the contribution not as "hedging demand explains AI valuations" but as "hedging demand is a *distinct, additional* channel that operates on top of growth and is identifiable because it responds to singularity risk rather than earnings expectations." This is what the testable-implications section already argues—let the framing in the introduction and conclusion match. Additionally, the paper could strengthen the quantitative case by showing calibrations where the hedging share is larger (e.g., higher $\gamma$, higher $\phi_L$) while remaining empirically plausible, and discussing what parameter values would make the hedging channel first-order.

2. **The model has constant $\lambda$, but the empirical narrative relies on time-varying perceived singularity risk.** Section 3.4 and Figure 2 argue that AI valuation spikes at GPT-3 and ChatGPT/GPT-4 milestones are "consistent with the hedging channel, in which a perceived increase in singularity risk (not just revised earnings forecasts) bids up AI stocks." The proposed formal test would "regress the AI premium on a proxy for perceived singularity risk." But the model provides no mechanism for $\lambda$ to change—it is a fixed parameter. This means the model's sharpest testable implication (the AI premium co-moves with perceived singularity risk, conditional on earnings) is not actually a prediction *of the model*; it is a prediction of an informal story told alongside the model. For a top finance journal, this gap matters. A natural extension would introduce Bayesian learning about $\lambda$: the household observes signals (AI capability advances, expert surveys) and updates its belief $\hat{\lambda}_t$. This would generate time-varying risk premia, sharper event-study predictions around AI milestones, and a tighter link between the formal model and the empirical patterns in Section 3.4. The learning extension need not be complex—even a conjugate-prior setup with rare discrete signals would suffice—but its absence leaves the paper's most distinctive empirical implication without formal support.
