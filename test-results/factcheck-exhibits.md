2. **Non-AI P/D ratios also increase with p** (from 9.8 to 13.3), which might seem counterintuitive since non-AI stocks are "displaced."
3. **Ratio column values.** The ratio of AI to non-AI P/D rises from 1.1 to 2.0 across the grid.

### Code check

1. **AI P/D increase with p:** The AI P/D is computed via exact backward recursion (`compute_pd_ai_exact`, lines 51–80). At each theta step, the AI dividend growth factor is `gamma_ai_k = (theta + dtheta*(1-theta))/theta * (1+eta)`. At theta=0.15: gamma_ai = (0.15 + 0.17)/0.15 × 1.5 = 3.2. The SDF-weighted dividend growth `S * gamma_ai` = (0.5^(-4)) × (1.5^(-4)) × 3.2 = 16 × 0.1975 × 3.2 = 10.11, which is large. Higher p puts more weight on this favorable singularity state, raising P/D. The backward recursion correctly iterates over the chain of post-singularity theta values, accounting for the fact that theta rises after each singularity. **Verified as correct.**
2. **Non-AI P/D increase with p:** The non-AI dividend growth factor is gamma_non = (1 - theta - dtheta*(1-theta))/(1-theta) × (1+eta) = 0.8 × 1.5 = 1.2. The SDF-weighted term is S × gamma_non = 16 × 0.1975 × 1.2 = 3.79, which exceeds 1. So the singularity state contributes positive value even for non-AI stocks (the SDF spike from displacement outweighs the dividend share loss for moderate parameters). Higher p thus raises non-AI P/D too, but less than AI P/D. **Verified as correct—not an error.**
3. **Ratio values:** Manually verified: at p=0.5%, ξ=0%, AI PD=15.5, non-AI PD=11.1, ratio=15.5/11.1=1.396, which rounds to 1.4. At p=1%, ξ=0%, ratio=26.5/13.3=1.992, which rounds to 2.0.
