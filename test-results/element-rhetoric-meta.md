### 1. Is the rhetorical device used in both the abstract and introduction? — PASS

- **Abstract** (final sentence): "This paper demonstrates the displacement it models: AI agents produced all analysis and writing from a human-authored specification."

Both instances are present and clearly invoke the self-referential device.

### 2. Would humans be turned off by the use of the rhetorical device? — PASS

The abstract usage is direct and prominent—it is the closing sentence—but it is framed as thesis-supporting evidence ("demonstrates the displacement it models") rather than as a boast or disclosure. This intellectual framing transforms what could be an off-putting admission into an argument for the paper's relevance. The introduction usage is in a footnote, which is maximally subtle and unlikely to distract readers who are not interested. The combination of one brief abstract sentence plus one footnote is well-calibrated: readers who find the device interesting can engage with it, while those who are skeptical can move past it quickly.

### 3. Is the use of the rhetorical device compelling and interesting? — PASS

The self-referential quality is genuinely clever: a paper about AI displacement of labor income that is itself produced by AI agents displacing academic labor. This is not a gimmick—it is direct evidence for the paper's thesis. The paper models how AI shifts consumption from households to AI owners, and the paper's own production process demonstrates exactly this dynamic in the domain of academic research. The device adds intellectual depth rather than merely decorating the paper.

### 4. Is the use of the rhetorical device distracting or overbearing? — PASS

The device appears exactly twice: once in the abstract (one sentence) and once in a footnote in the introduction. It is not mentioned in the model, extensions, or conclusion. This restraint prevents the device from overshadowing the economic content. The footnote placement in the introduction is particularly well-chosen—it signals that the self-referential point is a secondary observation rather than the paper's main contribution.
