## Per-question assessments

**Q1: Does the figure show that the singularity is catastrophic if the government does nothing?**
No. In panel (b), at tax rate = 0% (no transfers), the "Large singularity" line starts at roughly 5x consumption growth — already a large positive number. A reader would conclude the household is doing very well even without transfers, the opposite of the intended message. There is no visible sense of crisis or harm at the zero-tax intercept for the singularity scenario.

**Q2: Does the figure show that transfers are ineffective under normal conditions?**
Partially. The baseline (eta = 0.5) line is nearly flat and hugs the "No change" line across all tax rates, conveying that redistribution under normal conditions barely moves the needle. However, the message would be stronger if the baseline line dipped below "No change" at moderate tax rates to show that deadweight losses actively make things worse. As it stands, transfers look roughly neutral rather than clearly wasteful.

**Q3: Does the figure show that singularity-scale growth changes the calculus?**
The large-singularity line in panel (b) is steeply upward-sloping in the tax rate, suggesting transfers help more when growth is large. However, because the line already starts very high (~5x) at zero tax, the impression is that transfers make a good situation even better — not that they rescue the household from a bad outcome. The key insight that transfers are needed because the singularity is otherwise harmful to labor is lost.

**Q4: Is the overall message clear to a finance-journal reader?**
No. A reader would likely interpret the figure as: "The singularity is great for households regardless, and transfers just add a bonus on top." The figure needs to show a clear contrast — the household is worse off under the singularity at zero transfers, and transfers flip the outcome. That contrast is absent.
