# Referee Report: Hedging the AI Singularity

*Critical Finance Review. MS-32567*

## Summary and Assessment

This paper develops a simple consumption-based asset pricing model (CCAPM) featuring investors' needs to hedge AI displacement risk, which the author names the "AI Singularity." In the model, the representative household faces a systematic AI-induced disaster risk that substantially reduces the household's consumption growth. The household can hedge this risk by investing in AI stocks. This mechanism produces the key prediction that AI stocks have high price-dividend ratios due to investors' hedging demand.

In my view, this paper asks a timely question regarding how investors' concerns about AI affect asset prices. However, I find the economic contribution of the model in this short paper to be largely subsumed by prior literature on similar topics, primarily by Gârleanu, Kogan, and Panageas (2012 JFE), *GKP* hereafter. Below, I comment on the contribution and also provide suggestions for the author to differentiate the paper from the literature by exploring new features of AI Singularity. Another highly innovative feature of this paper is that it is written by generative AI software, with proper prompting and guidance from the author. The author deserves credit for producing one of the first research papers in such an innovative way.

## Comments

1. The model in this paper is largely subsumed by that of GKP.

Prior research has extensively studied investors' concerns about displacement risk, where new technologies displace investors' skills and reduce their income. A study highly relevant to this paper is GKP, which develops a model where existing investors similarly face displacement risk from innovative firms in the future. The key difference is that GKP goes one step further: In the current paper, existing investors can directly purchase AI stocks to fully hedge the displacement risk. Therefore, there is only one source of systematic risk in this model—consumption risk. In contrast, GKP assumes that the displacement risk cannot be fully hedged by existing investors, as the rents from innovation are captured largely by "future cohorts of firms" that haven't entered the economy yet. Therefore, GKP includes two sources of risk: the classic consumption risk and displacement risk. The best an existing

investor can do to hedge displacement risk is to purchase growth stocks, which provide only a partial hedge.

I view GKP as subsuming the intuition of the model in this paper for two reasons. First, the model in this paper resembles a special case of GKP's model in which we allow growth firms to fully hedge displacement risk (rather than partially hedge the risk). In that case, GKP's model collapses from a two-factor model to a single-factor CCAPM, which then yields the same prediction through a similar mechanism as this paper. Second, I find GKP's characterization of AI displacement risk more realistic than that in this paper. This paper assumes that AI stocks are already available for investors to purchase. However, much of the advancement in AI is driven by young startups like OpenAI that did not exist or trade publicly several years ago. Hence, the partial hedging setting in GKP appears more realistic. In this case, one could simply analyze the hedge effects on AI stocks in the same framework as GKP, just as they derive the hedge effects on growth stocks. Overall, the strong overlap between the models in this paper and GKP substantially reduces the contribution of this paper.

2. Suggestion: Dive deeper into the impact of AI Singularity.

AI Singularity is a much more profound and pervasive event than the simple displacement risk modeled in the current paper. A growing body of research has discussed the provocative impact of an AI Singularity event and the heterogeneous views people hold about it. For example, *Jones (2024)* discusses the heterogeneous views of rich and poor households on the existential risk from AI Singularity, highlighting that rich people are more concerned about AI Singularity, while poor people are more willing to gamble with their lives to attain much higher living standards. This feature relies on the assumption that existential risk strikes rich and poor equally once AI reaches Singularity. Incorporating such a feature could enrich the current paper's setting and predictions, where the displacement risk from AI Singularity disproportionately benefits AI asset holders while harming the rest of the population. Integrating these new aspects of AI Singularity appears to be a fruitful direction for expanding the model and distinguishing it from existing literature.
