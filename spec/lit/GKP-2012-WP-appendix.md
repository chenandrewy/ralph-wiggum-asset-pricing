### Proof of Proposition 1.

Consider the intermediate-good markets. We first derive the demand curve of the final-good firm for the intermediate input $j$ at time $t.$ Maximizing (6) with respect to $x_{j,t}$, we obtain

$x_{j,t}$ $=$ $L^{F}_{t}\left[\frac{p_{j,t}}{\omega_{j,t}Z_{t}\alpha}\right]^{\frac{1}{\alpha-1}}.$ (42)

Substituting this expression into (8) and maximizing over $p_{j,t}$ leads to

$p_{j,t}$ $=$ $\frac{w_{t}}{\alpha},$ (43)

while combining (42) and (43) yields

$x_{j,t}$ $=$ $L^{F}_{t}\left[\frac{w_{t}}{\omega_{j,t}Z_{t}\alpha^{2}}\right]^{\frac{1}{\alpha-1}}.$ (44)

Next, consider the labor markets. Maximizing (6) with respect to $L^{F}_{t}$ gives the first-order condition

$w_{t}L^{F}_{t}$ $=$ $\left(1-\alpha\right)Y_{t}.$ (45)

Substituting (44) into (3) and then into (45) and simplifying yields

$w_{t}=\left(\alpha^{2}\right)^{\alpha}\left(\frac{1-\alpha}{1+\chi}\right)^{1-\alpha}Z_{t}A^{1-\alpha}_{t}.$ (46)

We substitute equation (46) into (44) and then into (17) to obtain

$x_{j,t}$ $=$ $\frac{1+\chi}{A_{t}}\left(\frac{j}{A_{t}}\right)^{\chi}\frac{\left(1-\phi\right)\alpha^{2}}{\alpha^{2}+1-\alpha},$ (47)
$L^{F}_{t}$ $=$ $\frac{1-\alpha}{\alpha^{2}+1-\alpha}\left(1-\phi\right).$ (48)

Aggregate output is given by (19), which we derive by combining (47) and (48) inside (3). The fact that $\pi^{F}_{t}=0$ is an immediate implication of constant returns to scale in the production of final goods. Equation (20) is obtained by combining (47) and (43) with (8) and (46). Total wages $w_{t}\left(1-\phi\right)$ equal $\left(\alpha^{2}+1-\alpha\right)Y_{t},$ which follows from (46) and (19). Finally, integrating (20) to obtain $\int_{0}^{A_{t}}\pi^{I}_{j,t}dj$ implies that aggregate profits equal $\alpha\left(1-\alpha\right)Y_{t}.$

**Proof of Lemma 1.** To establish that the equity premium is non-zero in the limit, it suffices to show that

$$
\lim_{\alpha \to 1} \operatorname{cov} \left\{ R_{t}, \left( \xi_{t + 1} / \xi_{t} \right) \right\} \neq 0. \tag{66}
$$

Since $\kappa = 1$, all stocks have rate of return

$$
R_{t} = \frac{ \left( \pi_{t + 1}^{I} / \pi_{t}^{I} \right) + \left( \Pi_{t + 1} / \pi_{t + 1} \right) }{ \Pi_{t} / \pi_{t} }.
$$

Equation (57) implies that $(\Pi_t / \pi_t)$ is a constant. Therefore, in order to establish (66) it suffices to show that $\lim_{\alpha \to 1} \operatorname{cov}\left( \left( \pi_{t + 1}^{I} / \pi_{t}^{I} \right), (\xi_{t + 1} / \xi_{t}) \right) \neq 0$. To see that this is the case note

39

that $\lim_{\alpha \to 1}\left(\pi_{t + 1}^{I} / \pi_{t}^{I}\right) = e^{\mu -(1 + \chi)u_{t + 1}}$. Hence, in order to establish (66), we need to show that $\xi_{t + 1} / \xi_t$ is a non-degenerate function of $u_{t + 1}$ as $\alpha \rightarrow 1$. Given that

$$
\lim _ {\alpha \rightarrow 1} \left(\frac {\xi_ {t + 1}}{\xi_ {t}}\right) = \beta (1 - \lambda) ^ {\gamma} e ^ {\mu (- 1 + \psi (1 - \gamma))} \left[ 1 - \theta^ {w} (1 - (1 - \lambda) (1 + \delta) e ^ {- \rho u _ {t + 1}}) \right] ^ {- \gamma}, \tag {67}
$$

the lemma holds as long as a solution $\theta^w &gt; 0$ exists to Equation (50), an equation that simplifies to

$$
\begin{array}{l} \theta^ {w} \left(1 - \zeta (1 - \lambda) (1 + \delta) E \left[ e ^ {- \rho u} \left(1 - \theta^ {w} (1 - (1 - \lambda) (1 + \delta) e ^ {- \rho u _ {t}})\right) ^ {- \gamma} \right]\right) \\ = 1 - \zeta E \left[ \left(1 - \theta^ {w} (1 - (1 - \lambda) (1 + \delta) e ^ {- \rho u _ {t}})\right) ^ {1 - \gamma} \right]. \tag {68} \\ \end{array}
$$

By expanding the right-hand side of (68), the equation further simplifies to

$$
1 = \zeta E \left[ \left(1 - \theta^ {w} (1 - (1 - \lambda) (1 + \delta) e ^ {- \rho u _ {t}})\right) ^ {- \gamma} \right]. \tag {69}
$$

As the right-hand side increases in $\theta^w$, and the probability of the event $\{u_t \in (0, \epsilon)\}$ is strictly positive for all $\epsilon &gt; 0$, conditions (26) and (27) are necessary and sufficient for the existence of a solution $\theta^w &gt; 0$. (Note that $\theta^w \leq 1$.)

Proof of Lemma 4. The value of a growth firm is given by the value of all its assets in place and all its growth options.

$$
\begin{array}{l} P _ {t, s} = (1 - \eta) \kappa \left(\int_ {A _ {s - 1}} ^ {A _ {s}} \Pi_ {j, t} ^ {I} d j\right) + \sum_ {n = s + 1} ^ {t} (1 - \varpi) (1 - \kappa) \varpi^ {n - (s + 1)} \left(\int_ {A _ {n - 1}} ^ {A _ {n}} \Pi_ {j, t} ^ {I} d j\right) \\ + (1 - \varpi) \left[ E _ {t} \sum_ {n = t + 1} ^ {\infty} \left(\frac {\xi_ {n}}{\xi_ {t}}\right) \varpi^ {n - (s + 1)} \left(\int_ {A _ {n - 1}} ^ {A _ {n}} \Pi_ {j, n} ^ {I} d j\right) (1 - \kappa) \right] \\ \end{array}
$$

Using the definition of $\Phi$, and noting that $\int_{A_{s-1}}^{A_s} \pi_{j,t}^I dj = \left( \frac{A_s}{A_t} \right)^{1+\chi} \left( 1 - e^{-(1+\chi)u_s} \right)$ along with the definition of $\theta^g$ in equation (59) leads to (70).

The gross return on a growth firm $R_{t+1}^g$ at time $t+1$ is given by sum of the time-$t+1$ dividends from all the blueprints collected by the firm up to and including period $t+1$, $\alpha(1-\alpha)Y_{t+1}N_{t+1,s}$, and the end-of-period price $P_{t+1,s}$, divided by the beginning-of-period price $P_{t,s}$:

$$
R_{t+1,s}^g \equiv \frac{\alpha(1-\alpha)Y_{t+1}N_{t+1,s} + P_{t+1,s}}{P_{t,s}}.
$$

Using the definitions of $R_{t+1}^a$ and $R_{t+1}^o$, as given in the statement of the lemma, the gross rate of return on any growth firm can be expressed as in equation (33), where $w_{t,s}^o$ is the relative weight of growth opportunities in the value of the firm, and is obtained from Lemma 4 as

$$
w_{t,s}^o = \frac{(1 - \varpi)\varpi^{t-s}\theta^g\Phi}{(\Phi-1)N_{t,s} + (1 - \varpi)\varpi^{t-s}\theta^g\Phi}.
$$

Combining (20) and (19) with (31) and (32), we obtain that the return on assets in place has a negative loading on the innovation shock, $\frac{\partial R_{t+1}^a}{\partial u_{t+1}} &lt; 0$, while the return on the growth-opportunity component of the firm value has a positive loading, $\frac{\partial R_{t+1}^o}{\partial u_{t+1}} &gt; 0$.

Proof of Lemma 3. Equation (36) implies that

$$
a_{s+1}^i - a_s^i = \log c_{s+1,s+1}^i - \log c_{s,s}^i + \frac{1}{\gamma} \log \left(C_{s+1}^{(1-\psi)(1-\gamma)} \beta^{-(s+1)} \xi_{s+1}\right) - \frac{1}{\gamma} \log \left(C_s^{(1-\psi)(1-\gamma)} \beta^{-s} \xi_s\right). \tag{71}
$$

Using (53) inside (71) along with $C_s = Y_s$ and simplifying gives

$$
a_{s+1}^i - a_s^i = \log c_{s+1,s+1}^i - \log c_{s,s}^i - \log \left(\frac{C_{s+1}}{C_s}\right) - \log \left[ \frac{1}{1-\lambda} v(u_{s+1}; \theta^e, \theta^w, \theta^g) \right].
$$

Using the definitions of $a_s$ and $z_s$, (55), and (60), noting that $C_s = Y_s$, and simplifying gives

$$
a_{s+1} - a_s = - \log \left[ \frac{1}{1 - \lambda} v(u_{s+1}; \theta^e, \theta^w, \theta^g) \right] + z_{s+1} - z_s. \tag{72}
$$

Equation (39) follows immediately.

## B Data Description

The CEX data are from the NBER website as compiled by Ed Harris and John Sabelhaus. See http://www.nber.org/ces_cbo/Cexfam.pdf for a detailed description of the data. In

short, the data set compiles the results from the four consecutive quarterly interviews in the CEX into one observation for each household. We follow a large literature (see, e.g., *Vissing-Jorgensen (2002)*) and drop from the sample households with incomplete income responses, households who haven’t completed one of the quarterly interviews, and households that reside in student housing. To ensure that data selection does not unduly affect the results, we also ran all the regressions on the raw data including dummies for reporting status and the number of completed interview quarters. The results were not affected in any essential way.

In terms of our definition of consumption, we followed *Fernandez-Villaverde and Krueger (2007)* and used a comprehensive measure of consumption expenditure. Specifically, we used exactly the same definition as Harris and Sabelhaus. Our choice is motivated by our model; according to the model, cohort effects are determined by the intertemporal budget constraint of agents born at different times, so that total consumption expenditure seems to be the appropriate concept for the estimation of cohort effects. To test if this choice materially affects our conclusions, we also ran the results using consumption of non-durables and services instead of total consumption expenditure. Using consumption of non-durables and services, the volatility of first differences of cohort effects was larger; however, there was not a big difference between the variance of the permanent components of the cohort effects, no matter which concept of consumption we used, consistent with the view that the two estimates of cohort effects share the same stochastic trend.
