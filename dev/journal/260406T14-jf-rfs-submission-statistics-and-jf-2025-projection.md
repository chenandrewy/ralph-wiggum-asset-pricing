JF 2025 projection

Observed partial 2025 data from the AFA page:
- Monthly `Submissions` are posted for `January-October 2025`.
- These sum to `944`.
- Monthly `Resubmissions` for `January-October 2025` sum to `125`.
- Monthly `Total` for `January-October 2025` is therefore `1,069`.

Projection method:
- I used the public `2021-2024` JF monthly data on the AFA page.
- For each year, I computed `January-October` submissions and then measured how much `November-December` added.
- For `Submissions`, the implied `November-December` increment over `January-October` was:
- `2021`: `205 / 948 = 21.6%`
- `2022`: `158 / 968 = 16.3%`
- `2023`: `213 / 929 = 22.9%`
- `2024`: `151 / 786 = 19.2%`
- Applying those historical end-of-year seasonal increments to `January-October 2025 submissions = 944` gives:
- Low case using `2022` seasonality: about `1,098`
- High case using `2023` seasonality: about `1,160`
- Midpoint / average-seasonality estimate: about `1,130`

Projection takeaway:
- A reasonable full-year `2025` projection for JF `Submissions` is about `1,130`, with a rough range of `1,100-1,160`.
- That would put `2025` above `2024`, about in line with `2022`, and still below `2021` and `2023`.
- The same exercise for `Total` submissions including resubmissions gives a midpoint estimate of about `1,283` for full-year `2025`.

Interpretation:
- JF `Submissions` by year are:
- `2021`: `1,154`
- `2022`: `1,127`
- `2023`: `1,142`
- `2024`: `937`
- `2025` projection: about `1,130`
- On the public numbers, `2024` is clearly below `2021-2023` for JF whether one uses `Submissions` or `Total`.
- The partial `2025` monthly data suggest a rebound relative to `2024`, but not a dramatic break from the pre-2024 JF range.
- For RFS, the posted counts look roughly flat from the `2023` to `2024` editorial-year windows and then down in `2025`.

Top 5 economics journals: recent public evidence and the AI-submissions question

Question of interest:
- The narrower question is whether `ChatGPT` and related tools, which became widely used after `November 30, 2022`, appear to have caused a rise in submissions to leading economics journals in the `2023-2025` period.

What I could verify publicly:
- `Review of Economic Studies` has an editors report page stating that the journal now receives `close to 1,600 submissions per year`.
- Source: <https://academic.oup.com/restud/pages/editors_report>
- `Econometrica` has a public annual reports page with exact older counts, including:
- `2019-2020`: `1,205` new submissions
- `2020-2021`: `1,258` new submissions
- Source: <https://www.econometricsociety.org/publications/econometrica/annual-reports>
- For `American Economic Review`, `Quarterly Journal of Economics`, and `Journal of Political Economy`, I found official journal pages and editor-report landing pages, but I did not find publicly accessible, exact `2023-2025` submission totals that I could verify from the journal sites.
- `American Economic Review` editor-report landing pages:
- <https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pandp.114.734>
- <https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pandp.115.743>

Interpretation for the post-ChatGPT period:
- I did not find public Top 5 economics journal data showing a clear `2023-2025` surge in submissions attributable to `ChatGPT`.
- `ReStud` provides a current approximate level, but not a dated year-by-year jump for `2024` or `2025`.
- `Econometrica` provides verified historical evidence of rising submissions over time, but the public reports I found do not extend to exact `2024` or `2025` counts.
- For `AER`, `QJE`, and `JPE`, the recent public record is too thin to support a precise claim either way.

Related public discussion:
- Long-run evidence does indicate that Top 5 economics journal submissions increased substantially over prior decades.
- Card and DellaVigna summarize Top 5 submissions as having nearly doubled from `1990` to `2012`, with most of the increase occurring after `2000`.
- Source: <https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/nine-facts-about-top-journals-economics>
- I did not find persuasive `X` or other informal web discussion with concrete recent numbers showing that `ChatGPT` caused a discrete `2023-2025` break in Top 5 economics journal submissions.

Bottom line:
- The public evidence currently supports a claim of rising long-run submissions pressure in economics journals.
- It does not currently support a strong claim that `ChatGPT` caused a sharp increase in `2023-2025` submissions to Top 5 economics journals.
- The closest defensible summary is that `LLM` use in economics research and writing may be rising, but a corresponding post-`ChatGPT` submissions surge is not clearly visible in the recent public journal statistics I could verify.
